# Scottish Approach to Service Design (SAtSD)

The Scottish Approach to Service Design is the Scottish Government's framework for designing public services in a way that actively involves citizens and communities. It applies across national and local government, health, third sector, and cross-agency partnerships.

Its core vision: *"The people of Scotland are supported and empowered to actively participate in the definition, design and delivery of their public services — from policy making to live service improvement."*

This is not a project delivery methodology like Agile or PRINCE2. It is an approach to design and participation that can sit alongside any delivery method.

---

## The 7 Principles

### 1. Problem First
Explore and define the problem before designing a solution. Resist brief-taking at face value. Use discovery and framing work to confirm you are solving the right problem.

### 2. People-Centred
Design service journeys around people's actual lives — not around organisational structures, internal processes, or what is convenient to deliver. The person is the unit of design, not the service line.

### 3. Early Public Engagement
Involve the public from day one — not just in testing, but in shaping the research questions, interpreting findings, and generating solutions. Late-stage consultation is not participation.

### 4. Inclusive Methods
Use research and design methods that enable people to participate fully and meaningfully, regardless of literacy, digital access, language, disability, or other barriers. Design for the hardest-to-reach participant, not the most convenient.

### 5. Standardised Tools
Use the core set of tools and methods from the SAtSD framework — journey maps, service blueprints, personas, co-design methods — to enable shared understanding and knowledge transfer across organisations.

### 6. Knowledge Sharing
Share and reuse research insights, service patterns, and design components wherever possible. Avoid duplicating discovery work that has already been done by another part of the system.

### 7. Community Building
Continuously develop the SAtSD methods, tools, and practitioner community. Design practice improves through shared learning — contribute to the community as well as drawing from it.

## Life Events as a Design Lens

One of the most powerful concepts in the SAtSD is **life events** — major transitions in a person's life that require them to navigate multiple services across multiple organisations simultaneously.

Examples of life events:
- Having a baby
- Starting school
- Losing a job
- Being diagnosed with a serious illness
- Losing a close relative
- Becoming a carer
- Experiencing homelessness

During these moments, people don't experience "the NHS", "the Council", and "the DWP" as separate systems — they experience them as a single, often confusing and fragmented journey. The SAtSD asks organisations to step outside their own boundaries and design around the whole experience.

**Practical application:**
- Scope journeys from the life event, not the service entry point
- Map all the organisations a person must interact with during the event
- Identify the gaps, duplications, and friction points between organisations
- Focus design energy on the cross-organisational seams — the handoffs between systems that no single organisation owns

## Designing Across Organisations

Multi-agency service design is fundamentally different from single-organisation design. The challenges are structural, not just experiential.

### Common multi-agency friction points

| Issue | Manifestation |
|-------|--------------|
| Fragmented data | Person must repeat their story to each organisation |
| Misaligned processes | Organisation A's timeline doesn't match Organisation B's |
| Accountability gaps | Nobody owns the overall experience |
| Inconsistent eligibility criteria | Person falls between services |
| Poor information sharing | Referrals get lost; warm handoffs don't happen |
| Different digital maturity | One organisation has no digital capability |

### Principles for cross-organisational design

**Map the whole system first.** Before designing any one part, map all the organisations involved in the life event and how they connect. A stakeholder ecosystem map and a multi-organisation journey map are essential starting points.

**Identify the experience owner.** Someone needs to be accountable for the end-to-end journey, even if individual services are delivered by different organisations. Establish this role explicitly at the outset.

**Design the handoffs.** The transitions between organisations are where experience quality collapses. Design each handoff as explicitly as you design each service interaction: what information transfers, who communicates to the person, what the person experiences at the moment of transition.

**Work at the systems level, not just the service level.** Improving one organisation's part of the journey without addressing the system often makes things worse — it creates a speed mismatch or a new bottleneck at the next handoff.

**Use shared artefacts.** A single journey map that spans all organisations is more powerful than separate maps from each organisation's perspective. It forces shared understanding and reveals the seams.

## The SAtSD Maturity Assessment

The maturity framework helps organisations and partnerships understand where they are in adopting the Scottish Approach, and identify priority areas for improvement.

### The Five Maturity Levels

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Awareness** | Beginning to work in a participatory way; limited design practice |
| **Interested** | Understanding how the approach applies to their context |
| **Growing** | Actively building capacity and capability |
| **Strengthening** | Establishing consistency; developing strategic frameworks |
| **Thriving** | Organisation-wide, systematic use of this way of working |

### The Five Assessment Dimensions

#### 1. Engagement with SAtSD Principles, Tools, Methods and Community

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Awareness | No evidence of engagement with design methods, tools, or community |
| Interested | Some limited design practices; minimal awareness of the SAtSD |
| Growing | Design awareness emerging in parts of the organisation; community engagement growing |
| Strengthening | Design principles evident at many organisational levels; regular community engagement |
| Thriving | SAtSD principles understood organisation-wide; strong design leadership |

#### 2. Capacity and Capability for SAtSD

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Awareness | Heavy reliance on external expertise; minimal internal capability |
| Interested | Continued external dependency; limited ability to manage design-aligned projects |
| Growing | Emerging in-house research capacity; externally procured work well-managed |
| Strengthening | Growing internal capability; design professionals present; dedicated resources allocated |
| Thriving | Strong in-house design teams with executive-level design leadership |

#### 3. Focus on People and Their Lives

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Awareness | Services designed around organisational needs; minimal user research |
| Interested | Organisational needs take priority over user understanding |
| Growing | Initial understanding of diverse perspectives; limited ongoing engagement |
| Strengthening | Strong focus on diverse perspectives; continuous improvement culture |
| Thriving | Sustained, early focus on people's lives; aligned cross-service collaboration |

#### 4. Enabling People's Participation in Research and Design

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Awareness | Engagement limited to consultation; minimal service user involvement |
| Interested | Users researched upon; late-stage prototype testing only |
| Growing | Service users engaged in some project stages; participation planning evident |
| Strengthening | Full engagement in most stages; pockets of consistent good practice |
| Thriving | Services fully designed with — not just for — service users and staff |

#### 5. Inclusion and Accessibility for Design Participation

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Awareness | Little attention to accessible, inclusive design activities |
| Interested | Methods often inaccessible; importance of diversity poorly understood |
| Growing | Some inclusive practices; accommodations inconsistent |
| Strengthening | Mostly inclusive methods; diversity understood; training provided |
| Thriving | Fully inclusive and accessible for all design participants; sustained capacity building |

### Using the Assessment

1. Work through each dimension as a team or partnership — discuss rather than score individually
2. Be honest: assess where you are, not where you aspire to be
3. Identify the 1–2 dimensions where improvement would have the greatest impact
4. Set specific, achievable goals for each priority dimension
5. Reassess at regular intervals (typically annually for a programme; at key milestones for a project)

**What "proportionate" means:** The level of maturity that is appropriate depends on the size of the project, the complexity of the service, and the capacity available. Not every organisation needs to reach "Thriving" in every dimension. The goal is continuous improvement, not perfection.

## Participation From Day One

The SAtSD is explicit that participation must happen from the beginning — not just in testing.

**What early participation looks like:**
- Involving people with lived experience in framing the research questions
- Co-producing the discussion guide for discovery interviews
- Including community members in synthesis sessions to check interpretation
- Sharing early concepts with service users before internal review

**Why it matters:**
Late-stage involvement (testing a prototype before launch) is better than nothing, but it often means the fundamental design decisions have already been made. Involving people early changes those decisions — which is the point.

## Relationship to Other Frameworks

| Framework | Relationship |
|-----------|-------------|
| Double Diamond | SAtSD uses the Double Diamond as its design process model |
| Agile / Scrum | Complementary — SAtSD governs design approach; Agile governs delivery rhythm |
| GDS Service Standard | Closely aligned; SAtSD adds stronger participation and cross-agency emphasis |
| PRINCE2 | SAtSD is not a project management framework — it can sit within any PM approach |

## Level Guidance

**Practitioner:** Use the 7 principles as a project health check at each phase gate. Ask: "Are we genuinely problem-first here? Are we involving people early enough? Are our methods accessible?"

**Lead / Design Manager:** Use the maturity framework to have honest conversations with your organisation or clients about where they really are. Identify the one dimension that would unlock the most progress and focus resource there.

**Programme / Strategic level:** Use the life events lens to scope programmes that cross organisational boundaries. Resist the pull to design each organisation's component in isolation. Establish an experience owner for the whole journey before any design work begins.
