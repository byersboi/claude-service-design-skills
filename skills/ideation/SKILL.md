# Ideation

Ideation is the divergent phase of design — generating a wide range of possible responses to a defined problem before converging on the most promising concepts to develop. In the Double Diamond, ideation sits at the opening of the Develop phase.

The quality of what you deliver is determined by the quality of your ideas. Generating ideas well requires specific conditions, techniques, and structure — left unguided, groups produce incremental ideas from familiar territory.

---

## Principles of Good Ideation

**Quantity before quality.** The first idea is rarely the best idea. Volume creates the conditions for breakthrough. Aim to generate 50–100 ideas before evaluating any.

**Defer judgement.** Evaluation kills generativity. Separate divergent and convergent activities completely. No "yes but" during brainstorming.

**Build on others' ideas.** The best ideas often emerge from combining or extending another person's idea. "Yes, and..." not "yes, but..."

**Wild ideas welcome.** An impossible idea often contains a seed of a good one. Encourage people to push beyond the obvious. The impossible idea can be tamed; the obvious idea cannot be made interesting.

**Stay grounded in the user.** Every idea should be traceable to an insight from research. Ideation without research produces solutions to imaginary problems.

---

## Preparation: How Might We Questions

The single most important input to an ideation session is a well-crafted set of How Might We (HMW) questions.

HMW questions reframe insights and pain points as design opportunities. They are specific enough to be actionable but open enough to allow varied responses.

**Formula:** "How might we [verb phrase that opens up possibility] for [user] [so that/without] [outcome]?"

**From insight to HMW:**

| Insight | HMW question |
|---------|-------------|
| "Patients feel anxious because they don't know how long they will wait" | How might we give patients a sense of progress and control during their wait? |
| "Carers struggle to navigate multiple services for the same person" | How might we reduce the coordination burden on carers managing complex care needs? |
| "Users abandon the form because they don't understand the language" | How might we make the application process feel achievable for people with lower literacy? |

Prepare 3–6 HMW questions per ideation session. Too many dilutes focus; too few limits variety.

## Ideation Techniques

### Brainstorming

**When to use:** Warm-up, rapid idea generation, groups of 3–8.

**How to run:**
1. Post the HMW question prominently
2. Set ground rules: no evaluation, build on ideas, quantity, wild ideas welcome
3. Generate silently for 3–5 minutes first (prevents groupthink)
4. Share and build on ideas as a group
5. Capture every idea — no idea is too small or too strange

**Duration:** 20–40 minutes

---

### Crazy 8s

**When to use:** Breaking out of the first obvious idea; individual creativity; pairs.

**How to run:**
1. Each participant folds an A4 sheet into 8 panels (or uses a digital template)
2. Set a timer for 8 minutes
3. Sketch one idea per panel — 8 ideas in 8 minutes
4. No words — sketch and annotate only
5. Share back in pairs, then small groups

**Why it works:** Speed prevents over-attachment to any one idea. The constraint forces people past their first obvious response.

**Duration:** 8 minutes ideation + 10 minutes sharing

---

### SCAMPER

**When to use:** Developing and extending existing ideas; avoiding idea fatigue.

SCAMPER is a checklist of transformations to apply to an existing idea or service:

| Letter | Prompt | Question |
|--------|--------|---------|
| S | Substitute | What if we replaced [component] with something else? |
| C | Combine | What if we combined this with [another service/product]? |
| A | Adapt | What from a different industry could be adapted here? |
| M | Modify / Magnify | What if we exaggerated one element? What if it was 10x larger/faster/simpler? |
| P | Put to other uses | What else could this be used for? |
| E | Eliminate | What if we removed [component] entirely? |
| R | Reverse / Rearrange | What if we reversed the order? What if the user did this part instead of the staff? |

**Duration:** 30–45 minutes (work through 3–4 letters)

---

### Worst Possible Idea

**When to use:** When a group is stuck, risk-averse, or defaulting to incremental thinking.

**How to run:**
1. Ask the group to generate the worst, most useless, most harmful ideas they can think of
2. Capture all of them
3. Then reverse each: "If the worst idea is [X], what would be the opposite?"
4. The reversals often contain genuinely good ideas

**Why it works:** Removes the pressure of generating "good" ideas. People relax and become more creative. The reversal step frequently surfaces ideas that would not have emerged otherwise.

**Duration:** 20–30 minutes

---

### Analogous Inspiration

**When to use:** When the group is stuck in category thinking; early in an ideation session.

**How to run:**
1. Identify a service challenge (e.g., "managing anxiety during a long wait")
2. Ask: "What other industries or contexts handle this well?" (e.g., theme park queues, airline boarding, hospital pre-op rooms)
3. Research or discuss how those contexts handle the challenge
4. Extract principles and translate them to your context

**Duration:** 30 minutes including research or discussion

---

### Round Robin

**When to use:** Ensuring equal contribution; groups with seniority imbalance.

**How to run:**
1. Each person takes a turn to add one idea
2. No skipping; no evaluation
3. Continue until ideas are exhausted
4. Everyone contributes equally regardless of seniority

**Duration:** 20–30 minutes

## Concept Development

After generating a long-list of ideas, converge on the most promising and develop them into concepts.

### From idea to concept

An idea is a single thought: "What if we sent reminders by text?"
A concept is a developed proposition: "A proactive notification system that sends personalised updates to patients 48 hours, 24 hours, and 2 hours before their appointment, with a one-touch confirmation that reduces no-show rates."

**Concept card format:**

```
Concept name: [Short, memorable name]
Tagline: [One sentence that captures the essence]
Description: [3–5 sentences: what it is, how it works, what problem it solves]
Key features: [3–5 bullet points]
How it addresses the HMW: [Explicit link back to the design challenge]
What we'd need to test: [Key assumption that needs validating]
[Rough sketch or visual]
```

### How many concepts?

Develop at least 3 distinct concepts before evaluating any. This prevents falling in love with the first idea and ensures genuine variety.

---

## Prioritisation and Convergence

### Dot voting

Each participant gets 5–10 sticky dots (physical or digital). Vote for the ideas or concepts you find most promising. No need to explain votes.

After voting, a rough prioritisation emerges. Use it as a starting point for structured discussion — not as a final decision.

### Effort / Impact Matrix

Plot concepts on axes of effort to implement vs. likely impact on the user experience. Focus development energy on high-impact, lower-effort concepts first.

### Design criteria check

Before selecting a concept for development, check it against the design criteria established in Define:
- Does this address the core insight?
- Is it feasible given our constraints?
- Does it work for the user we are designing for?
- Does it avoid creating new problems?

## Common Mistakes

**Evaluating too early.** The most damaging thing in an ideation session is "yes, but..." in the generative phase. If it happens, name it and redirect to the ground rules.

**Generating solutions without HMW questions.** Ideation without a defined challenge produces solutions looking for problems. Always start with HMW questions rooted in research insights.

**Selecting the first idea.** Confirmation bias leads groups to select the first reasonable idea. Force at least three concepts into development before evaluation.

**Only the loudest voices contribute.** Use silent generation, dot voting, and round-robin to counteract this.

**Not documenting ideas.** Ideas generated in workshops and not captured are lost. Photograph everything. Write up the long-list even before prioritisation.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Learn to write strong HMW questions — they are the most important facilitation skill in ideation. Practice converting research insights into HMW questions before every session.

**Mid-weight:** Focus on managing the diverge/converge rhythm. Know when to close down brainstorming and move to prioritisation — and push back when the group wants to jump to convergence too early.

**Senior / Lead:** Design for variety in your concept set. If all three concepts are evolutionary rather than transformational, add a fourth explicitly constrained to be "radical" — even if it seems impractical. The tension between evolutionary and radical ideas often generates the best final concepts.
