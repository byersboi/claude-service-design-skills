# Prototyping (Service Design)

In service design, a prototype is any rough representation of a service concept that can be tested with users. Unlike product or software design, service prototypes often involve people, spaces, and processes — not just screens.

The purpose of prototyping is to learn, not to present. A prototype that confirms your assumptions has not been tested — it has been demonstrated. A good prototype surfaces surprises, failures, and mismatches between your intent and the user's experience.

**Prototype early, prototype rough.** The cardinal rule. The more polished a prototype, the less honest feedback it receives. Rough prototypes invite criticism; polished ones invite compliance.

---

## Fidelity Levels

Fidelity describes how closely a prototype resembles the final service.

| Level | Description | When to use |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Low fidelity** | Rough sketches, props, role play, paper | Early in Develop — testing whether the concept makes sense |
| **Medium fidelity** | Structured role play, digital mockups, scripted interactions | Mid-Develop — testing key interactions and flows |
| **High fidelity** | Close to final service, real systems, live pilot | Late Develop / Deliver — validating the full experience before launch |

**Default to lower fidelity than feels comfortable.** Teams routinely invest too much in prototypes before they know what they are testing. A paper sketch can answer whether a concept is understandable in 30 minutes. A polished prototype takes days to build and produces little additional learning at that stage.

## Prototyping Methods

### Paper Prototyping

Drawing service interactions, interfaces, or spaces on paper.

**Best for:** Digital touchpoints, forms, letters, notifications, app flows.

**How to run:**
1. Sketch each screen or step on a separate piece of paper or card
2. One person plays the "computer" — swapping cards in response to user actions
3. Observe how the user interprets and interacts with each step
4. Ask aloud what they expect to happen, not just what they do

**What to test:** Does the layout make sense? Do users understand the language? Do they find what they are looking for? Where do they get stuck?

---

### Desktop Walkthrough

Simulate a service using small-scale physical props — like a model train set or a diorama — to represent spaces, objects, and actors.

**Best for:** Services with significant spatial or physical components; service flows that involve handoffs between multiple actors.

**Materials:** Printed cards for artefacts and touchpoints, small figures or post-it "actors", rough printed service environments.

**How to run:**
1. Set up the service environment on a table using props
2. Walk through the service scenario step by step, narrating the customer and staff actions
3. Pause at each touchpoint to discuss what works and what does not
4. Use observers to note reactions and questions

**What to test:** Does the flow make logical sense? Where are the handoffs? Where does the physical experience break down?

---

### Role Play / Experience Prototyping

Simulating the service by having team members, stakeholders, or actual users act out the service interactions.

**Best for:** Services that are primarily human interactions — consultations, advice services, care, retail.

**How to run:**
1. Define the scenario: who the customer is, what they need, what context they are in
2. Assign roles: customer, service employee, any other actors (manager, carer, etc.)
3. Brief the person playing the customer on their character — but do not script their responses
4. Run the interaction, staying in role as far as possible
5. Debrief immediately: what worked? What was surprising? What broke down?

**Variants:**
- **Bodystorming:** Physically inhabiting a space and moving through it as a user. Reveals spatial and environmental design opportunities.
- **Experience prototype with real users:** Recruit actual users to role play interactions with service staff or prototyped touchpoints. Higher validity than internal role play.

**What to test:** How does the emotional quality of the interaction feel? What scripts and responses work well? Where does the staff member not know what to say?

---

### Storyboard

A sequence of illustrated frames showing a user's journey through the service scenario.

**Best for:** Communicating a concept clearly to stakeholders; testing whether the overall narrative of the service makes sense.

**Format:** 6–12 panels, comic-book style. Each panel shows a moment in the journey. Stick figures are fine — visual quality is irrelevant.

**Elements per panel:**
- Scene (where are they?)
- Character action (what are they doing?)
- Dialogue or thought bubble (what are they saying or thinking?)
- Annotation (what is the key design element here?)

**How to test:** Show the storyboard to a user and ask them to narrate what is happening. Where they misinterpret the sequence, the concept needs clarification.

---

### Wizard of Oz

A technique where a human manually performs what would eventually be automated — without the user knowing.

**Best for:** Testing digital interfaces or AI-driven services before building the technology.

**Classic example:** Testing a chatbot by having a human researcher read incoming messages and type responses in real time, while the user believes they are interacting with a bot.

**How to run:**
1. Build a simple front-end interface that the user interacts with
2. A "wizard" behind the scenes manually performs the system's function
3. Test with users as if the system were real
4. Learn what users expect, what language they use, and where they get confused

**What to test:** What inputs do users give? What outputs do they expect? What confuses them? Is the concept viable?

---

### Service Advertisement / Concept Video

A short (60–90 second) video or poster presenting the service as if it already exists.

**Best for:** Testing whether the core value proposition is compelling and understood.

**How to test:** Show the concept video to potential users. Ask: Does this seem useful? Would you use this? What questions does it raise? What are your concerns?

---

### Pilot

A live, bounded test of the full service in a real environment with real users.

**Best for:** Late Develop / Deliver phase — validating the full service model before wider rollout.

**Design the pilot carefully:**
- Define clear success criteria in advance
- Choose a bounded geographic or demographic scope
- Identify what will and won't be included in the pilot
- Build in explicit measurement and feedback mechanisms
- Plan the de-escalation or rollback process

**What to test:** Does the full service model work in real conditions? What operational issues emerge? What is the staff experience? Do outcomes improve?

## Planning a Prototyping Session

### Before the session

1. **Define the test question:** What specific assumption are you testing? "We believe users will understand the new triage process" is testable. "We want to see if the concept works" is not.

2. **Choose the right prototype type:** Match fidelity and method to the question and the phase.

3. **Prepare materials:** Build only what is needed to answer the test question — not a complete prototype.

4. **Recruit participants:** Real users where possible. Internal colleagues are useful for logistics rehearsal, not for real learning.

5. **Define roles:** Facilitator (runs the session), player (acts as service in role play), observer (takes notes), timekeeper.

### During the session

- Brief participants: "We are testing this concept, not testing you. There are no wrong answers."
- Ask participants to think aloud as they interact with the prototype
- Observe — take notes on what they do, not just what they say
- Ask probing questions after each step: "What did you expect to happen there?"
- Resist the urge to explain or defend the prototype when users struggle

### After the session

- Debrief within 30 minutes — capture what you observed while it is fresh
- Distinguish between: what users said, what users did, and what you infer from both
- Identify: what worked, what did not, what you would change
- Plan the next prototype iteration based on what you learned

## Common Mistakes

**Building too much before testing.** If you've spent more than a day building a prototype for a first test round, you've probably overbuilt.

**Testing with the wrong people.** Internal team members will not give you the same responses as real users. Invest in recruiting even 3–5 real users.

**Explaining the prototype when users get confused.** When a user doesn't understand something, the instinct is to explain. Resist. The confusion is the data.

**Testing the prototype, not the concept.** Users should interact with the prototype naturally. If you're walking them through it step by step, you're demonstrating, not testing.

**Not iterating.** A prototype that is not iterated is a demonstration. The cycle — build, test, learn, rebuild — is the point.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Default to lower fidelity than feels comfortable. The discomfort of showing rough work to real users is the most important thing to get over early in your practice. Start with paper and role play.

**Mid-weight:** Design your test questions with precision. Vague test questions produce vague insights. "We want to know if users like it" tells you nothing useful. "We want to know whether users understand the difference between Plan A and Plan B at the point of decision" is testable.

**Senior / Lead:** Champion prototyping in the project process. Many organisations move from research directly to delivery — bypassing prototyping entirely. Advocate for dedicated prototyping and testing time. The cost of testing a rough prototype for two days is negligible compared to the cost of fixing a fully implemented service that does not work.
