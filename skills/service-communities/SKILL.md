# Service Communities

Service communities are a model for designing and delivering government services that span multiple departments, agencies, or organisations. They address one of the most persistent structural problems in public service design: the fact that the services users need rarely align with the organisational boundaries of government.

The concept was developed at the Government Digital Service and piloted from 2017 onwards.

---

## The Problem Service Communities Solve

Users do not experience government as departments. They experience it as tasks — "start a business", "learn to drive", "export animals", "develop a medical condition and stop working". Each of these tasks cuts across multiple organisations:

- **Start a business** touches DWP, BEIS, DfE, HMRC, DIT, Companies House, FSA, The Pensions Regulator, British Business Bank, IPO, and MHCLG
- **Import or export something** involves 36 separate organisations
- **Develop a medical condition and stop working** crosses 3 departments

From the user's perspective, this is one service. From government's perspective, it is dozens of separate systems, each owned by a different team with different funding, governance, technology, and culture.

> "The structure of government isn't designed to meet user needs."

This structural mismatch is not a failure of individual teams — it is a design problem at the level of government itself. Service communities are a practical response to that problem.

---

## What Is a Service Community?

> "Semi-formal networks of people with different skills that work across departments and agencies to design and deliver end-to-end services."
> — Government Digital Service

**Key characteristics:**

- **Oriented around a service**, not an organisation — defined by what the user is trying to do, not by which team "owns" it
- **Multi-functional** — includes policy, operations, digital, data, research, and technology professionals
- **Collaborative across governance boundaries** — funding and reporting lines remain separate; the community creates a shared working layer above them
- **Delivery focused** — exists to improve the service for users, not to produce reports or strategies

---

## What a Service Community Provides

**For the service:**
- A focus on improving the whole service as users experience it, not just individual touchpoints
- A structure for identifying and resolving the joins between organisations that users experience as friction

**For the people in it:**
- Space and time to share knowledge cross-functionally
- A rhythm for ongoing collaboration between teams that would otherwise rarely speak
- A forum for continual learning and improvement

**For the organisation:**
- A practical mechanism for making services "organisationally agnostic" — hiding internal structures from users
- A route toward the principle that services should shape government, not the other way around

## How Service Communities Work in Practice

### Formation

Service communities typically begin with a mapping exercise: who is involved in delivering this service end-to-end? This often surfaces organisations that were not previously aware of each other's role in the user journey.

The GDS model used workshops as the initial convening mechanism:
- Import-Export: 1 workshop, 10 departments/agencies, 31 people
- Start a Business: 8 workshops, 10+ departments/agencies, 25+ people
- Employ Someone: 3 workshops, 6 departments/agencies, 15 people

The first workshop typically has two goals: establishing a shared understanding of the end-to-end service from the user's perspective, and surfacing the seams where users currently fall through the gaps.

### Structure

Service communities are deliberately "semi-formal" — they have enough structure to sustain ongoing work, but not so much that they require new governance, new budgets, or formal sign-off to exist. This is by design: formal structures take time to establish and can be blocked by the same organisational politics they are trying to cut across.

In practice, a service community typically has:
- A **convener** — someone with enough cross-organisational credibility to call the group together and maintain momentum (often from GDS, a shared services team, or a lead department)
- A **regular rhythm** — monthly or quarterly working sessions
- A **shared artefact** — a service map, user journey, or shared backlog that the group maintains together
- **Representatives from each key organisation** — not necessarily the most senior people, but people with enough understanding of their organisation's role to speak for it and enough authority to act on decisions

### What Service Communities Do

The work of a service community depends on the maturity of the service, but typically includes:

1. **Mapping the whole service** — creating a shared picture of the end-to-end journey that everyone can use as a reference point
2. **Identifying seams** — places where the service hands off between organisations, and users experience inconsistency, repetition, or dead ends
3. **Prioritising improvements** — agreeing which seams cause the most user harm and are most tractable to fix
4. **Coordinating change** — each organisation makes changes within their own domain, but the community coordinates timing, testing, and communication to ensure the whole service improves together
5. **Sharing standards and patterns** — agreeing on shared language, shared data standards, and shared design patterns to reduce the friction at joins

## Barriers to Working This Way

Understanding the barriers helps service communities work around them rather than being surprised by them.

### Funding and governance silos
Each organisation has its own budget cycle, approval process, and set of metrics. Changes that benefit the whole service may not benefit any individual organisation's metrics. A service community cannot override governance — it has to make the case for change within each organisation's own terms.

**Response:** Make the case for each organisation in their own language. "Fewer failed applications reaching your team" is more compelling than "better user experience" for an operations manager.

### Siloed professions
Policy, digital, operations, and technology teams rarely share a working environment even within a single organisation. Across organisations, the separation is greater. People in different professions may not know enough about each other's constraints to collaborate effectively.

**Response:** Build cross-functional literacy into the community from the start. Structured tours of each other's working environments and decision-making processes help people understand why things are the way they are before trying to change them.

### Technology and contracts
Legacy technology, long-term contracts, and complex integration dependencies make it genuinely difficult to change how data flows between systems. Some joins that cause user friction exist because of technical constraints that cannot be changed quickly.

**Response:** Separate the design intent from the delivery timeline. Agree on what the service should do for users, and design toward that even if some improvements take years. Short-term improvements (clearer handoff communications, better signposting, shared contact numbers) can reduce harm while technical changes are underway.

## Outputs of Service Community Work

Service communities typically produce a small number of high-leverage outputs:

**Service maps** — shared, maintained pictures of the end-to-end service that all participating organisations contribute to and refer back to. More valuable than any individual team's process map because they show the joins.

**Step-by-step navigation** — a user-facing guide to the whole service journey (like GOV.UK's "Get your business ready to employ someone") that consolidates navigation across multiple departments. This can often be delivered before any backend integration work is done.

**Shared language** — agreed terminology for the service that all organisations use in their user-facing communications, reducing confusion at handoffs.

**Seam register** — a maintained record of the joins between organisations, with the user harm caused by each, and the status of any improvement work.

## When to Propose a Service Community

Consider proposing a service community when:

- **User research shows handoff failures** — users repeatedly encounter dead ends or have to re-enter information when they move between parts of the service
- **Multiple organisations are producing competing or inconsistent guidance** — users receive different answers from different parts of government about the same topic
- **A "start-to-finish" service cannot be owned by one team** — the task crosses too many governance boundaries for any single team to take end-to-end ownership
- **Improvements are blocked by coordination problems** — individual teams want to improve their part but cannot make the change without another team doing something first

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** The most important contribution at this level is thorough service mapping — tracing every step of the user journey and identifying which organisation is responsible for each step. Map the handoffs carefully. A precise picture of the joins is the foundation everything else is built on.

**Mid-weight:** Focus on the quality of the relationships between organisations, not just the outputs. Service communities depend on trust between people who have no formal obligation to work together. Building those relationships through genuine curiosity about each organisation's constraints — not just advocacy for the user — is the difference between a productive community and a polite stalemate.

**Senior / Lead:** The hardest work at this level is making the case for the community's existence to each organisation's leadership. Each senior sponsor needs to understand what their organisation gains, not just what the user gains. Frame improvements in terms of operational benefit — reduced support contacts, fewer failed applications, lower error rates — as well as user experience. The political case for service communities must be made continuously, not just at the start.
