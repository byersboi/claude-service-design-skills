# Stakeholder Map

A stakeholder map identifies everyone with an interest in, or influence over, a service or design project — and helps determine how to engage them. In service design, stakeholders include not just end users but employees, managers, commissioners, regulators, and partner organisations.

Stakeholder mapping is typically done early in a project (Discover phase) to inform research planning, and revisited throughout to manage engagement and navigate organisational complexity.

---

## Who Is a Stakeholder?

A stakeholder is anyone who:
- **Uses** the service (directly or indirectly)
- **Delivers** the service (front-line staff, operational teams)
- **Commissions or funds** the service (budget holders, commissioners)
- **Regulates or governs** the service (regulators, policymakers)
- **Is affected** by the service outcomes (families, communities, partner organisations)
- **Can block or enable** change (senior leaders, unions, IT, legal)

## Mapping Techniques

### 1. Power / Interest Matrix

The most widely used tool for stakeholder analysis. Plot stakeholders on two axes:

```
          HIGH POWER
               │
  KEEP        │  MANAGE
  SATISFIED   │  CLOSELY
               │
───────────────┼───────────────
               │
  MONITOR      │  KEEP
               │  INFORMED
          LOW POWER
               │
          LOW INTEREST ────── HIGH INTEREST
```

**Manage closely (high power, high interest):** Key decision-makers with strong stakes. Engage deeply, frequently, and personally. These are the people who can champion or kill your project.

**Keep satisfied (high power, low interest):** Powerful stakeholders who are not closely interested in day-to-day decisions. Keep informed and consulted. If their interest increases, move them to "manage closely."

**Keep informed (low power, high interest):** Often front-line staff, end users, or community groups. Keep them informed and involved — they have deep knowledge and their buy-in matters for implementation.

**Monitor (low power, low interest):** Minimal engagement needed. Check in periodically. Be alert to changes in their interest or power.

### 2. Onion Diagram

Maps stakeholders in concentric circles by proximity to the service:

```
          ┌─────────────────────────┐
          │   WIDER ENVIRONMENT     │
          │  ┌───────────────────┐  │
          │  │  ORGANISATIONS    │  │
          │  │  ┌─────────────┐  │  │
          │  │  │   DIRECT    │  │  │
          │  │  │ STAKEHOLDERS│  │  │
          │  │  │  ┌───────┐  │  │  │
          │  │  │  │SERVICE│  │  │  │
          │  │  │  │  CORE │  │  │  │
          │  │  │  └───────┘  │  │  │
          │  │  └─────────────┘  │  │
          │  └───────────────────┘  │
          └─────────────────────────┘
```

**Service core:** The primary users and delivery team.
**Direct stakeholders:** Teams and partners directly involved in service delivery.
**Organisations:** Departments, partner organisations, commissioners.
**Wider environment:** Regulators, policymakers, media, community.

Useful for: Understanding system complexity, identifying who needs to be at the table, communicating scope to clients.

### 3. Stakeholder Wheel

A circular map placing the service at the centre with stakeholder groups radiating outward. Useful for visualising the breadth of stakeholders and making the map accessible to non-designers.

### 4. Influence / Impact Matrix

Variant of the power/interest matrix. Replaces "interest" with "impact" (how much the service affects them). Useful when designing for equity — highlights groups who are highly impacted but may have low power.

## Step-by-Step Process

### 1. Brainstorm all stakeholders

Start with a broad brainstorm. Use these prompts:
- Who uses this service?
- Who delivers this service?
- Who manages or funds this service?
- Who sets the rules this service must follow?
- Who is affected by this service even if they don't use it?
- Who could block or enable change to this service?
- Who has tried and failed to access this service?
- Who is currently excluded from this service?

Write each stakeholder on a separate sticky note. Aim for more names than you think you need — it is easier to merge or discard than to realise you have missed someone important.

### 2. Categorise stakeholders

Group into primary categories:
- **Users** — direct service users, including edge cases and underserved groups
- **Delivery staff** — front-line employees, contractors
- **Operations and management** — team leaders, operations managers
- **Commissioning and funding** — budget holders, commissioners, sponsors
- **Governance and compliance** — legal, risk, regulator, union
- **Partners and suppliers** — third-party organisations who deliver part of the service
- **Adjacent services** — services that users also interact with (often missed)
- **Community** — families, carers, community groups

### 3. Plot on a matrix

Place each stakeholder on the power/interest matrix. Discuss and adjust as a group — disagreements about where someone belongs reveal important assumptions to investigate.

### 4. Define engagement approach

For each quadrant, agree: who will engage this stakeholder, how often, through what channel, and what you need from them.

### 5. Identify gaps and risks

Ask:
- Who is missing from this map?
- Who is hardest to reach but most important?
- Who could derail this project if not engaged?
- Whose voice is not currently represented?

### 6. Revisit regularly

Stakeholder maps go stale. Revisit at each project phase. Power and interest shifts — a stakeholder who was low interest at the start may become critical when implementation begins.

## Engagement Strategy

| Quadrant | Frequency | Channel | What you need from them |
|----------|-----------|---------|------------------------|
| Manage closely | Weekly or fortnightly | 1:1 meetings, steering group | Decisions, sponsorship, unblocking |
| Keep satisfied | Monthly | Written updates, executive briefings | Buy-in, resource, sign-off |
| Keep informed | Regular touchpoints | Newsletters, workshops, drop-ins | Knowledge, operational insight, implementation readiness |
| Monitor | Quarterly | Newsletter, public updates | — |

## Common Mistakes

**Only mapping senior stakeholders.** Leadership buy-in matters, but projects fail when front-line staff are not engaged. Include operational stakeholders from the start.

**Treating the map as static.** Stakeholder dynamics change. A merger, leadership change, or regulatory shift can transform the map overnight. Review it at every phase transition.

**Confusing users and stakeholders.** Users are a specific type of stakeholder. Map them separately with user research tools (personas, journey maps). The stakeholder map focuses on organisational and systemic actors.

**Avoiding the difficult stakeholders.** The stakeholder who is hard to engage often holds critical knowledge or can block implementation. Identify them early and build a strategy for engaging them.

**Not including the people most affected but least powerful.** Equity-focused service design requires actively seeking out marginalised and underserved groups — they rarely appear in a standard power/interest mapping exercise.

## Using the Map for Research Planning

The stakeholder map directly informs who to include in research:

- **Users in the keep-informed quadrant** → prioritise for user research (interviews, contextual inquiry)
- **Delivery staff** → prioritise for process understanding (shadowing, staff interviews)
- **Manage-closely stakeholders** → stakeholder interviews for context, constraints, and organisational intent
- **Partner organisations** → interviews to understand interdependencies

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Focus on breadth first. It is better to over-populate the map and trim it than to miss important stakeholders. Use the brainstorm prompts systematically.

**Mid-weight:** Push for honesty on the power/interest ratings. Teams often underestimate the power of IT, legal, or procurement — who can all derail implementation even if they are not "decision-makers" in the traditional sense.

**Senior / Lead:** Use the stakeholder map as a political navigation tool. The "manage closely" quadrant is where you will spend most of your relational capital. Understand each person's priorities, pressures, and personal stake in the project before each interaction. Stakeholder management is a design skill, not a soft skill.
