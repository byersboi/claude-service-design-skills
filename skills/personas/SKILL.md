# Personas

A persona is a composite representation of a user type, grounded in research, that helps a team design with a specific person in mind rather than a vague, averaged user.

Personas work because specificity is generative. "Design for NHS patients" is too abstract to drive design decisions. "Design for Janet — a 68-year-old who travels by bus to outpatient appointments, has anxiety about medical settings, and needs to tell her daughter she arrived safely" creates a concrete context that generates specific, testable design decisions.

---

## Types of Persona

### Research-based Persona

Built from qualitative and quantitative research. Multiple interviews, observations, and data sources are synthesised into a composite character. The most reliable type — but requires significant research investment.

**When to use:** Projects with sufficient research time; when the team lacks empathy for the user group; when assumptions about users vary widely within the team.

### Proto-Persona

Built from team assumptions before research. Explicit about the fact that it is hypothesis-based, not evidence-based. Useful as a research planning tool and for aligning teams on who they think they are designing for.

**When to use:** Early in a project, before research; to surface and align assumptions within the team; when time does not allow full research-based personas. Always update with research once fieldwork is complete.

**Critical:** Label proto-personas explicitly as assumption-based. If they are treated as real, they do the same damage as designing from assumptions alone.

### Archetypes

Higher-level user types that represent patterns of behaviour rather than demographic segments. More useful than personas when the service must work across very different demographic groups.

**Example:** Instead of personas based on age or location, archetypes might be:
- **The navigator:** Confident, proactive, self-manages their experience
- **The reluctant user:** Disengaged or avoidant; approaches the service with low confidence
- **The advocate:** Manages the experience on behalf of someone else (carer, parent, support worker)

## Persona Components

### Core Components (every persona)

**Name and representative image**
Give the persona a specific name and a realistic photo or illustration. This creates the specificity needed for design decisions.

**Quote**
A single sentence in the persona's voice that captures their core attitude or goal. Distilled from research. "I just want to get this sorted without having to explain myself to three different people."

**Background**
Brief, relevant biographical context: role, life situation, relevant demographics. Keep only what is relevant to the service — not a full biography.

**Goals**
What does this person fundamentally want to achieve? Both functional (get the thing done) and emotional (feel in control, feel respected, not feel judged).

**Frustrations / Pain points**
What problems, frictions, and barriers does this person currently experience? Grounded in research.

**Behaviours**
How does this person actually behave? What are their habits and workarounds? This is often more useful than demographics.

**Technology and channel use**
How does this person interact with digital and non-digital channels? What are their capabilities and preferences?

### Optional Components

**Needs and motivations:** What drives this person's behaviour? What do they need beyond the functional?

**Fears and concerns:** What makes them anxious or reluctant about this service?

**A day in the life:** A short narrative or timeline of a typical day, showing where this service sits in their broader life.

**Relevant quotes:** 3–5 direct quotes from research that evidence the persona's characteristics.

## Empathy Map

An empathy map is a structured tool for capturing what a user says, thinks, does, and feels. Used in synthesis to build towards a persona, or as a standalone activity to build empathy within a team.

### Quadrants

```
         ┌─────────────────┬─────────────────┐
         │     THINKS      │      SAYS       │
         │                 │                 │
         │ Internal        │ Verbal          │
         │ thoughts,       │ expressions,    │
         │ unspoken        │ statements,     │
         │ concerns,       │ what they       │
         │ questions       │ tell others     │
         ├─────────────────┼─────────────────┤
         │      DOES       │     FEELS       │
         │                 │                 │
         │ Observable      │ Emotional       │
         │ actions,        │ state, tone,    │
         │ behaviours,     │ feelings        │
         │ habits          │                 │
         └─────────────────┴─────────────────┘
                  ↑                ↑
              PAINS           GAINS
```

**Says:** Direct quotes from research — what the person says aloud.
**Thinks:** What the person is thinking but may not say — inferred from behaviour, hesitation, and context.
**Does:** Observable actions and behaviours — what you see them doing.
**Feels:** The emotional quality of their experience — based on what they expressed and what you observed.
**Pains (below the map):** Frustrations, fears, and obstacles.
**Gains (below the map):** Goals, desires, and measures of success.

**How to build:**
1. Run an empathy mapping session after research synthesis
2. Write observations from research onto sticky notes, one per note
3. Cluster into the appropriate quadrant
4. Identify tensions between quadrants (e.g., "says they are fine" but "feels anxious")

## Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

Jobs to be done is an alternative framework for understanding user motivation. Rather than asking who the user is, it asks: what job is the user trying to get done?

**Formula:** "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

**Example:**
> "When I'm managing my mother's care needs, I want to understand all the options in one place, so I can make an informed decision without feeling like I've missed something."

JTBD is particularly useful when:
- Demographics don't explain behaviour (people of all ages and backgrounds use your service for different reasons)
- You want to focus design on motivation rather than user attributes
- The team is getting distracted by demographic segmentation

JTBD and personas are complementary. A persona can have multiple jobs to be done. A job can be shared across multiple personas.

## Step-by-Step Process: Research-Based Persona

### 1. Review your research data

Gather: interview transcripts, observation notes, survey data, analytics, complaints data.

### 2. Identify behavioural patterns

Look for clusters of similar behaviours, motivations, and attitudes — not demographic clusters. Behaviour is more useful than demographics for design.

Common behavioural dimensions:
- Confidence and digital capability (high ↔ low)
- Engagement with the service (proactive ↔ reactive)
- Prior experience (new ↔ experienced)
- Level of support available (well-supported ↔ isolated)

### 3. Define the persona types

Identify 2–4 distinct types. More than 4 is typically too many to hold in mind during design. Use behavioural clusters, not demographic averages.

### 4. Construct each persona

For each type:
- Write a name, background, goals, and frustrations
- Pull supporting quotes from research
- Build an empathy map as a mid-step
- Write a representative quote in the persona's voice

### 5. Validate

Where possible, review the persona with research participants — ask if they recognise themselves and their peers.

### 6. Use the persona actively

Personas have no value on a wall. Use them in design critiques ("Would Janet find this form understandable?"), in facilitation ("Let's walk through this scenario as Maria"), and in stakeholder conversations.

## Common Mistakes

**Designing from assumptions, calling it a persona.** Proto-personas are useful, but only if they are clearly labelled as hypotheses and updated with research. Assumption-based personas treated as truth are dangerous.

**Too many personas.** If you have more than 4 primary personas, simplify. Design cannot serve everyone equally. Prioritise the user who matters most, and make edge cases explicit.

**Demographic personas.** "18–35 year old urban professional" is a demographic segment, not a persona. Personas are grounded in behaviour, motivation, and context — not demographics.

**Personas no one uses.** Personas that live on a Miro board and are not referenced in design decisions are decoration. Integrate them into design reviews, critique sessions, and decision-making frameworks.

**Averaging out differences.** The most valuable insights often live in the edges — the user who struggles most, the outlier who uses the service in an unexpected way. Don't average these away into a smooth persona that describes no one precisely.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Always start with an empathy map before writing a persona. It forces you to separate what users said from what they think and feel — and makes the persona more grounded and nuanced.

**Mid-weight:** Challenge the team to refer to personas by name during design reviews. "Would Sarah understand this?" is a more useful question than "Would users understand this?" If the team can't answer, the persona is not being used effectively — or it needs more depth.

**Senior / Lead:** Introduce JTBD alongside personas when the service must work across diverse demographics. The combination gives you both the human specificity of personas and the motivational clarity of jobs.
