## Journey Stages

Define 4–8 high-level stages. Examples by service type:

**Transactional service:**
Awareness → Research → Decision → Purchase → Onboarding → Use → Support → Renewal / Exit

**Healthcare:**
Concern → Referral → Appointment booking → Waiting → Consultation → Treatment → Recovery → Follow-up

**B2B service:**
Problem awareness → Vendor research → Evaluation → Procurement → Implementation → Adoption → Review

**Government service:**
Life event occurs → Realises need for service → Finds the service → Applies → Waits → Receives decision → Complies / Appeals

## Emotional Curve Guidance

Plot a single line showing experience quality across all stages:

```
   +3  |         *
   +2  |      *     *
   +1  |   *              *
    0  |                     *
   -1  |
   -2  |
   -3  |
        [Stage 1][Stage 2][Stage 3][Stage 4][Stage 5]
```

**Scoring guide:**
- +3: Delightful — exceeds expectations, memorable moment
- +2: Good — meets expectations, smooth and easy
- +1: Acceptable — works but unremarkable
- 0: Neutral — neither positive nor negative
- -1: Frustrating — minor friction or confusion
- -2: Poor — significant problem, creates effort or distress
- -3: Unacceptable — causes serious harm, distress, or failure

**Moments to annotate:**
- Peak (highest point) — what is working well
- Nadir (lowest point) — the priority design opportunity
- Cliff edges — sudden drops in experience quality

## Pain Point Severity Rating

Use a consistent scale when annotating pain points:

| Severity | Definition | Action |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Critical | Causes task failure, distress, or abandonment | Prioritise in next sprint |
| High | Creates significant effort, frustration, or workaround | Address this quarter |
| Medium | Noticeable but manageable friction | Backlog — address when capacity allows |
| Low | Minor irritation, rarely mentioned | Monitor — fix if quick win |

## Common Stage Structures

### E-commerce
| Stage | Typical actions | Common pain points |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| Awareness | Sees ad, social post, or recommendation | Ads feel irrelevant, discovery is random |
| Research | Reads reviews, compares products | Too much information, hard to compare |
| Decision | Adds to cart, looks for discount | Shipping costs revealed late |
| Purchase | Enters payment details, confirms order | Trust concerns, too many steps |
| Waiting | Tracks delivery, receives notifications | Inaccurate tracking, unclear ETAs |
| Receive | Unpacks, uses product | Packaging hard to open, poor instructions |
| Support | Returns or exchanges | Complex returns process |

### Healthcare appointment
| Stage | Typical actions | Common pain points |
|-------|----------------|-------------------|
| Concern | Notices symptom, decides to seek help | Uncertainty about severity |
| Access | Calls GP, books appointment | Long hold times, limited slots |
| Pre-visit | Receives reminder, prepares | Unclear what to bring |
| Arrival | Parks, finds clinic, checks in | Wayfinding, long queues |
| Wait | Sits in waiting room | No information about wait time |
| Consultation | Speaks with clinician | Short time, information overload |
| Post-visit | Receives results, follows up | Unclear next steps, long wait for results |

## Journey Map Quality Checklist

Before sharing or presenting a journey map:

- [ ] Each stage is named and has clear start/end boundaries
- [ ] Actions are grounded in research (not assumptions)
- [ ] Touchpoints are specific (not just "digital" or "in-person")
- [ ] Thoughts are phrased in the first person ("I...")
- [ ] Emotions are specific (not just "happy" or "sad")
- [ ] The emotional curve is present and legible
- [ ] Pain points are distinguished from opportunities
- [ ] At least 3 opportunities are identified
- [ ] Evidence source is noted for key claims
- [ ] The map has been reviewed with at least one research participant
