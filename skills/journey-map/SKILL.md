# Journey Map

A journey map visualises the end-to-end experience of a person as they interact with a service — capturing their actions, thoughts, emotions, pain points, and opportunities at each stage. It is one of the most used artefacts in service design because it creates shared empathy and alignment across teams.

A good journey map is grounded in research. A journey map built from assumptions alone is a story about what your team thinks happens — which is often wrong in important ways.

---

## Types of Journey Map

**Current-state journey map**
Maps the experience as it exists today. Used in the Define phase to synthesise research and reveal pain points. The starting point for most service design work.

**Future-state journey map**
Maps the intended experience after redesign. Used in the Develop and Deliver phases to articulate and align on the target experience. Should be paired with a future-state service blueprint.

**Day-in-the-life map**
Zooms out beyond the service interaction to show the broader context of a person's day. Useful for services that are embedded in daily routines or for identifying when not to interrupt the user.

**Service journey map**
Maps multiple journeys across different user types interacting with the same service. Useful for complex services where different users (patient and carer, customer and employee) have interdependent journeys.

---

## Components

### Core rows (every journey map)

**Stages / Phases**
The high-level chapters of the journey. Typically 4–8 stages. Keep them broad enough to be legible from a distance. Examples: Awareness → Research → Decision → Onboarding → Use → Support → Exit.

**Actions**
What the person does at each stage. Specific, observable, grounded in research. Use active verbs: "Searches Google", "Calls helpline", "Fills in form", "Waits for confirmation".

**Touchpoints**
The channels and interfaces through which the person interacts with the service at each stage. Examples: website, mobile app, phone, in-person, letter, email, SMS, retail outlet.

**Thoughts**
What the person is thinking at this stage. Their questions, assumptions, and mental models. "Is this the right option for me?", "Why hasn't anyone called me back?", "I don't understand this form."

**Emotions / Feelings**
How the person feels at this stage. Often shown as a curve (positive to negative) across the top or bottom of the map. Grounded in quotes and observations from research — not in what the team thinks the customer should feel.

### Optional rows (add as relevant)

**Pain points**
Explicit problems, frustrations, and barriers experienced at this stage.

**Opportunities**
Design opportunities that emerge from pain points. Often phrased as How Might We questions.

**Channels / Systems**
Which internal systems or channels are involved at this stage (useful for connecting to a blueprint).

**Quotes**
Direct quotes from research that evidence the thoughts and emotions. Powerful for stakeholder communication.

**Metrics**
Existing quantitative data that corresponds to this stage: drop-off rates, completion rates, complaint volumes, NPS scores.

## Step-by-Step Process

### 1. Define scope

Before starting, agree:
- **Who** — which persona or user segment you are mapping (one per map)
- **What** — which service or sub-service
- **Scope** — where the journey begins and ends (err towards starting earlier than you think — awareness and decision often reveal the most important problems)

### 2. Gather research

Assemble: interview notes, observation recordings, diary study outputs, survey data, complaints and feedback, analytics. Do not start mapping without at least some primary research.

### 3. Define stages

Draft 4–8 high-level stages. Test these by checking: do your actions cluster naturally within each stage? If a stage contains more than 8–10 actions, split it. If it contains fewer than 3, merge it.

### 4. Map actions and touchpoints

For each stage, list every action the person takes and the channel it happens through. Ground these in observation — what they actually do, not what they are supposed to do.

### 5. Add thoughts and emotions

Layer in what the person is thinking and feeling at each stage. Use direct quotes where possible. The emotional curve should be drawn as a single line showing the rise and fall of experience quality — this makes the journey legible at a glance.

### 6. Identify pain points and opportunities

Annotate pain points explicitly. Then generate opportunities: for each pain point, ask "How might we...?" and write the HMW question in the opportunities row.

### 7. Validate with research participants

Where possible, review the map with the people it describes. This surfaces errors and generates new insight.

## The Emotional Curve

The emotional curve is often the most powerful element of a journey map for stakeholder communication. It makes the highs and lows of the experience legible at a glance.

**Drawn as a line chart** running left to right across the map. High on the Y-axis = positive experience. Low = negative experience.

**Annotate the peaks and troughs.** The lowest points are the priority design opportunities. The highest points show what is working and should be protected.

**Ground it in evidence.** The curve should reflect what research participants said and showed — not the team's aspirations for how customers should feel.

---

## Common Mistakes

**Using assumptions instead of research.** The most common failure. A workshop-generated journey map built from team assumptions is a hypothesis — valuable, but label it as such and validate it with research before designing from it.

**Making it too detailed.** A journey map with 60 actions across 10 stages becomes unreadable. Aim for clarity over completeness. The goal is insight, not documentation.

**Mapping too narrow a scope.** Many services begin well before the customer touches the product. Awareness, research, and decision stages are often where the most important problems live — and are frequently cut.

**Forgetting the emotional layer.** Actions and touchpoints without emotions is a process map, not a journey map. The emotional layer is what connects the artefact to human experience and makes it compelling to stakeholders.

**Treating it as finished.** Journey maps are living documents. Update them as you learn more.

**One map for all users.** Different user segments have fundamentally different journeys. Create separate maps for distinct personas. Where journeys diverge, show the divergence — don't average it out.

## Workshop Approach

**Team-based current-state mapping (90 minutes):**
1. Brief the team on the persona and scope (10 min)
2. Individual sticky note generation — one action per note (15 min)
3. Cluster into stages as a group (20 min)
4. Add thoughts and emotions per stage (20 min)
5. Identify top pain points and opportunities (15 min)
6. Debrief — what is surprising? What needs validating? (10 min)

**Research-based journey mapping:**
Build the map in synthesis sessions following fieldwork. Use affinity mapping to cluster findings, then translate clusters into journey stages and populate the map layer by layer.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Start with the actions row before any other layer. Get the spine of the journey right first. Then add touchpoints, then thoughts and emotions. Don't try to fill every cell — honest gaps are better than invented content.

**Mid-weight:** Challenge the scope. Ask "Where does this journey really begin?" Most teams map too narrow a scope. Push the start point further back (before the customer actively engages with your service) to find the most important insights.

**Senior / Lead:** Use the journey map as a conversation tool, not a deliverable. A map shown to an executive in a workshop reveals more than the same map sent in a slide deck. The moment of shared understanding — when a stakeholder sees the customer experience clearly for the first time — is the most powerful thing a journey map can produce.
