# Embedding Service Design

Embedding service design in an organisation is not a project — it is a cultural change. The tools and methods of service design are relatively straightforward to teach. Getting an organisation to genuinely work in a service design way, consistently and over time, is a much harder problem. It requires vision, patience, and deliberate effort to build the conditions where design thinking can take root and spread.

---

## The Starting Point: Recognition

The first step is organisational recognition that service design has a place. This sounds simple, but it is often the hardest step. Many organisations use the language of design without committing the conditions that make it possible — time, resource, access to users, and permission to challenge the way things are currently done.

Genuine recognition requires:

- **Vision from the top** — senior leadership who understand what service design is and what it makes possible, not just in theory but in the context of their organisation's specific challenges
- **Business strategy alignment** — service design placed at the heart of how the organisation plans to improve, not as a separate team or initiative running alongside normal work
- **Structural commitment** — dedicated time, space, and people, not service design done in the margins of other roles

Without this foundation, service design tends to stay at the level of individual projects — useful but fragile, dependent on particular people and easily de-prioritised when other pressures arrive.

---

## Building a Common Language

A shared design vocabulary is the foundation upon which a service design culture is built. Before organisations can do service design consistently, they need to be able to talk about it consistently.

This matters because:

- **Design terms are often borrowed from other fields** — words like "user", "journey", "prototype", "service", and "research" are used differently in design contexts than in business or technology contexts. Teams can appear to agree while meaning very different things.
- **A common language enables consistent practice** — when people use tools and methods with a shared understanding of what those tools are for and how they work, the quality and consistency of the work improves significantly
- **It bridges business and design** — the most effective design vocabularies are not pure design jargon. They are built to be meaningful to the whole organisation, connecting design concepts to the business language people already use

Building a common language is not about producing a glossary. It is about creating enough shared experience of working in the same way — through workshops, projects, and practice — that the language emerges naturally and sticks.

### What this looks like in practice

- Introducing design concepts through doing, not training alone — workshops where people use the language in context are more effective than slide decks that explain it
- Starting with the terms that cause the most confusion — "user" versus "customer", "service" versus "product", "need" versus "requirement"
- Tailoring the vocabulary to the organisation — the right language for a local council is different from the right language for a financial services company or a government department

---

## How Design Thinking Spreads

Once a common language begins to take hold, something tends to happen that is hard to plan for but important to understand: design thinking starts to spread on its own.

When people have a shared vocabulary and positive experiences of working in a design way, several things happen:

- **Discussions become more meaningful** — conversations about services, users, and problems become more precise and more useful, because people are working from the same mental models
- **Tools and methods become easier to use** — when everyone understands what a journey map or a how-might-we question is for, facilitating these activities takes less explanation and produces better outputs
- **Staff carry what they have learned into new contexts** — people who have experienced service design in one project bring it to the next, and to their teams, and to the colleagues they talk to

This organic spread — design going viral — is one of the most powerful ways a culture embeds. It cannot be manufactured, but the conditions for it can be created: enough people having enough good experiences that the approach develops a life of its own inside the organisation.

## Keeping Management On-Board

One of the most common failure modes for embedding service design is losing management support after the initial enthusiasm fades. This happens when managers are not given the tools to understand and use service design themselves — when it remains something done by a specialist team rather than something woven into how the organisation works.

For service design to become part of the culture, managers need:

- **Time and space to develop their own awareness** — managers who have only been briefed on service design, without experiencing it, struggle to support their teams effectively. Involvement in workshops and working sessions matters.
- **Integration with their management practice** — the most effective managers use service design tools and methods as a natural part of how they plan, communicate, and make decisions, not as something separate from their "real" work
- **Guidelines and process support** — clear documentation of how service design fits into the organisation's existing processes makes it easier for managers to embed it as the normal way of working, not an exception

The goal is not for managers to become designers. It is for them to understand design well enough to create the conditions where designers — and everyone else — can work in a design way.

## Failure Is Part of the Journey

Embedding a service design culture takes time, and the path is not linear. Teams will run workshops that don't work. Projects will revert to old patterns when under pressure. Leaders who were supportive will move on. Vocabulary that seemed embedded will fade.

This is not failure — it is the nature of cultural change. The organisations that embed service design most successfully are the ones that treat setbacks as information rather than defeats. They ask what the failure revealed about the conditions that weren't yet in place, and they use that to improve the approach.

Some things that commonly go wrong:

- **Service design applied as a process rather than a mindset** — following the steps without genuine curiosity about users or willingness to change based on what is learned
- **Design teams isolated from delivery** — insights and recommendations that never connect to the decisions that affect the service
- **Embedding without permission to challenge** — creating the language and methods of service design inside an organisation that is not willing to hear what they reveal about how it is currently working

---

## Encouraging Tools to Evolve

A sign that service design is genuinely embedding is when the people using it start to adapt the tools. This is a feature, not a problem.

When staff are confident in the purpose and principles behind service design methods, they naturally modify them to fit the situations they encounter. A journey map template that works perfectly for a transactional digital service may need significant rethinking for a long-term care service. A co-design workshop format that works with confident urban residents may not work at all with vulnerable rural users.

This evolution should be encouraged:

- **Understand before adapting** — the most effective adaptations come from practitioners who have used the standard approach enough to understand what it is trying to achieve, and can preserve that intent while changing the form
- **Share adaptations across the organisation** — when one team finds a better way to use a tool for a particular context, that learning should be available to others
- **Treat the toolkit as a living thing** — the organisation's collection of tools and methods should grow and change as the people using them become more experienced

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Focus on building your own fluency with the vocabulary and tools before trying to spread them. The most effective advocates for service design are people who have used it enough to talk about it with genuine confidence and specific examples. Document what works and what doesn't — that evidence will be useful later.

**Mid-weight:** Your role is often to be the bridge — between design and business language, between specialist designers and non-designer colleagues, between the approach and the organisation. Focus on making service design accessible to people who are encountering it for the first time. Find the managers and senior people who are curious and give them good experiences. They will do more to spread the culture than any training programme.

**Senior / Lead:** The hardest work at this level is creating the structural conditions — ensuring design is embedded in strategy, that there is genuine leadership commitment, and that the organisation's processes support rather than obstruct design practice. This often means making the case for service design in terms of organisational outcomes, not design quality. Reduced failure demand, faster resolution of user problems, lower cost per transaction — these are the arguments that land with senior audiences who are not yet convinced.
