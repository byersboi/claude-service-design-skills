# Culture Change Indicators

Signs that a service design culture is genuinely taking root in an organisation — as opposed to service design being done by a specialist team in isolation.

---

## Early Signs (0–12 months)

- People outside the design team are using design vocabulary correctly and consistently in meetings
- Colleagues are asking to be involved in research and workshops rather than needing to be recruited
- At least one manager is using service design tools as part of their own planning and communication
- Teams are producing journey maps or service maps as a natural part of how they think through problems, not only when asked by a designer

---

## Developing Signs (1–3 years)

- Design approaches are referenced in business cases and strategy documents
- Non-designers are facilitating design workshops with confidence
- Teams are adapting tools to fit their context rather than applying them mechanically
- When something goes wrong in a service, the first instinct is to go back to users — not to find an internal fix
- New starters are being inducted into design methods as a normal part of how the organisation works

---

## Embedded Signs (3+ years)

- Service design is present in job descriptions and performance frameworks across roles, not only design roles
- Leadership actively uses design evidence — user research, service data, failure demand — in decision making
- The organisation has a distinctive design vocabulary that is recognisably its own, adapted from general practice to fit its specific context and services
- Design thinking has survived significant leadership changes — it is in the organisation, not in the people
- Teams routinely challenge service boundaries and organisational structures when user research reveals they are causing harm

---

## Warning Signs

Things that suggest service design is present in name but not yet embedded in practice:

- Design is only done at the beginning of projects ("the discovery phase") and not revisited during delivery
- User research findings that are uncomfortable are regularly set aside or reframed
- The same problems keep appearing in research, suggesting findings are not consistently informing change
- Design vocabulary is used in presentations but not in day-to-day working conversations
- Service design is described as a methodology rather than a way of thinking
- Designers are given the tools to work but not the permission to share what they find
