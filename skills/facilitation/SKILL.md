# Facilitation (Service Design)

Facilitation is the skill of designing and guiding group processes so that a team produces better outputs together than they would individually. In service design, facilitation underpins nearly every activity: research debriefs, synthesis sessions, ideation workshops, co-design sessions, and stakeholder alignment meetings.

Good facilitation is invisible. When it is done well, participants feel the session moved naturally and the outputs came from them — not from the facilitator.

---

## The Facilitator's Role

**The facilitator is not a subject matter expert** (or at least should not act like one during the session). The facilitator's job is to design and hold the process — the participants supply the content.

Core responsibilities:
- **Design** the session: objectives, activities, timing, energy management
- **Hold the space**: create psychological safety for honest participation
- **Guide the process**: move the group from divergence to convergence
- **Manage energy**: balance high-energy and reflective activities
- **Capture outputs**: ensure insights and decisions are documented
- **Stay neutral**: on content, while being directive on process

---

## Workshop Design Process

### 1. Define the objective

Before designing any activity, answer clearly:
> "At the end of this session, the group will have [output/decision/shared understanding]."

One clear objective per session. If you have three objectives, plan three sessions or ruthlessly prioritise.

### 2. Understand your participants

- How many people?
- What are their roles and level of seniority?
- How well do they know each other?
- What do they already know about the topic?
- What biases or tensions exist in the group?
- Is anyone likely to dominate? Is anyone likely to disengage?

### 3. Design the arc

Every good workshop has three phases:

**Open:** Build connection, establish safety, frame the purpose. Never skip this — even if you are short on time.

**Explore:** The main work of the session. Alternate between divergent (generating) and convergent (selecting) activities.

**Close:** Synthesise what was produced, clarify decisions and next steps, close the energy loop.

### 4. Plan each activity

For each activity, define:
- **Purpose:** What does this produce or enable?
- **Time:** How long including setup and debrief?
- **Instructions:** What do you say to set it up? (Write these out — in the moment, instructions often become unclear)
- **Materials:** What do participants need?
- **Output format:** What does it look like when done?

### 5. Plan for what goes wrong

- What if a key participant doesn't show up?
- What if the group gets stuck?
- What if one person dominates?
- What if you run out of time?
- What if the technology fails (remote sessions)?

## Essential Activities

### Opening Activities

**Check-in round:** Each person shares one word or sentence about how they are arriving. Quick, no pressure. Builds presence.

**Hopes and concerns:** Each person writes one hope and one concern for the session on sticky notes. Surfaces anxieties and creates implicit contracting about what the session needs to achieve.

**One true thing:** Each person shares one thing they know to be true about the topic. Surfaces existing knowledge and creates shared baseline.

**Warm-up question:** A low-stakes question unrelated to the work: "What would your superpower be?" Lowers defences and builds energy.

**Draw a present:** A physical icebreaker that demonstrates the core design shift from designing for yourself to designing for users — ideal for opening co-design sessions or introducing service design to a new audience.

*How to run it:*
1. Give everyone a sticky note and a marker. Ask them to draw a present in ten seconds. Most people draw a box with a ribbon — the same image. Point this out: drawing is a shared language; everyone understood the brief.
2. Ask them to draw the *perfect* present in ten seconds. People draw things meaningful to them — a book, a holiday, a pet.
3. Ask them to hand their drawing to the person next to them. Ask: "Is this their perfect present?"
4. Debrief: when you designed the perfect present, you designed it for yourself — not for the person receiving it. That is exactly what happens in service design when we build without involving users. The rest of today's session is about doing the opposite.

*Why it works:* It is fast, physical, and makes the abstract concrete. The moment of handing over the drawing — and the slight awkwardness of receiving something clearly not meant for you — lands the user-centred design principle better than any explanation.

### Generative Activities

**Brainstorming:** Divergent idea generation. Set clear rules: build on ideas, no evaluation, quantity over quality, wild ideas welcome. Use a prompt or HMW question as the stimulus.

**Crazy 8s:** Individual rapid sketching — 8 ideas in 8 minutes. Forces volume and breaks attachment to first ideas.

**Round-robin:** Each person adds one idea in turn, building on what was said before. Ensures equal contribution.

**Silent generation (brainwriting):** Everyone writes ideas silently before sharing. Prevents groupthink and gives introverts equal footing.

### Convergence Activities

**Dot voting:** Each participant gets a set number of votes (sticky dots or digital equivalent). Vote for the most promising ideas, pain points, or priorities. Fast, visual, democratic.

**2x2 prioritisation matrix:** Plot items on axes such as Effort/Impact or Desirability/Feasibility. Forces relative prioritisation and surfaces assumptions.

**Affinity mapping:** Cluster sticky notes by theme. Give themes names. Identify patterns. Used in synthesis sessions after research.

**Rose / Thorn / Bud:** Review a concept or experience. Roses = what is working. Thorns = what is not. Buds = what could be. Good for structured feedback.

### Closing Activities

**What / So what / Now what:** What did we produce? So what does it mean? Now what do we do next? Connects output to action.

**Action logging:** Final 10 minutes: who will do what by when. Be explicit — outputs do not become actions without this step.

**Plus/delta:** What should we keep (plus)? What should we change (delta)? Quick session retrospective.

## Managing Group Dynamics

### Dominating participants

- Use structured activities that require individual work before sharing (silent generation, round-robin)
- Say: "Let's hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet"
- In 1:1 break, ask them to help draw out others
- For persistent dominators: create a "parking lot" for their ideas and move on

### Silent or disengaged participants

- Design activities where everyone contributes individually (sticky notes, cards)
- Use breakout pairs or small groups — easier to speak in a pair than a room
- Direct questions: "What's your take on this, [Name]?"
- Check in privately during a break

### Conflict between participants

- Acknowledge the tension rather than avoiding it: "It sounds like there are two different views here — let's capture both"
- Separate the people from the ideas: "Both of those are interesting positions. Let's put them both up and work with them"
- Refer back to the session objective to refocus: "Our job today is to understand the problem, not to decide the solution"

### Hierarchy in the room

Senior seniority suppresses honest contribution. Mitigations:
- Individual silent generation before group sharing
- Anonymous sticky notes
- Small groups without the senior person present
- Explicit norms at the start: "All voices matter equally in this room"

## Remote Facilitation

**Use a digital whiteboard** (Miro, Mural, FigJam). Prepare the board fully before the session — participants should not be navigating a blank canvas.

**Assign a co-facilitator** for tech support and chat monitoring.

**Design for 20% more time.** Remote sessions need more breathing room between activities.

**Breakout rooms aggressively.** Pair work is more engaging than whole-group work in a video call.

**Camera norms.** Encourage cameras on. Normalise it at the start: "Seeing faces helps us stay connected — please keep cameras on if you can."

**Check connection at the start.** Have a low-stakes activity in the first two minutes that requires everyone to interact with the board (place a sticky note, dot vote on a warm-up question).

## Timing and Energy Management

**The 90-minute rule.** After 90 minutes of focused work, group cognition degrades. Plan a break or a major energy shift at this point.

**Alternate energies.** Move between high-energy (brainstorming, building) and reflective (reading, writing) activities. Sustained high energy burns out; sustained reflection disengages.

**Protect the close.** Sessions routinely overrun on content and sacrifice the close. The close is where outputs become actions. Guard it.

**Build in buffer.** Every activity takes longer than planned. Add 20–25% buffer to your total timing.

**Fewer activities, done well.** A common mistake — especially for new facilitators — is packing too many activities into the available time and cutting each one short. Three activities completed properly produce better outputs than six activities half-finished. When planning, ask: "If I could only do one activity in this session, which would it be?" Protect that one. Everything else is optional.

**When participants go off-topic.** Participants sometimes veer away from the task and into broader discussion about the topic. The instinct is to redirect immediately — but pause first. Tangential conversations occasionally surface a bigger, more important issue than the one you planned to address. Listen for 60–90 seconds before intervening. If the discussion is generating genuine insight, note it and create a brief space for it before returning to the task: "That's really important — let's capture that on a sticky note and come back to it. For now, let's finish this activity." If it is simply going in circles, redirect with energy: "Brilliant — let's take that as our starting point for the next activity."

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Focus on clear instructions. The most common facilitation failure is ambiguous activity instructions that leave participants confused. Write your instructions out word-for-word and practise them. Time your activities in advance.

**Mid-weight:** Focus on energy and pacing. Read the room constantly. Be prepared to cut activities, swap the order, or extend a conversation that is producing insight. The agenda is a plan, not a contract.

**Senior / Lead:** Focus on design. The quality of a workshop is determined before it begins — by the clarity of the objective, the mix of participants, and the activity design. Invest in workshop design, not just delivery. A well-designed session can be handed to a mid-weight facilitator. A poorly designed session cannot be saved by the best facilitator in the room.
