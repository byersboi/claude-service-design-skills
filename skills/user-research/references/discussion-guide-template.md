## Before You Start

**Interviewer notes:**
- Read the guide in full before the session — know it well enough not to be reading from it
- The guide is a map, not a script. Follow the conversation where it goes
- Your most important job is to listen. Aim to speak for no more than 20% of the session time
- Use silence. Wait a beat after each answer before responding — participants often add the most important thing after a pause
- Curiosity is your superpower. Be genuinely interested, not professionally polite

## Opening (10 minutes)

### Introducing yourself and the project

> "Thanks so much for making time today. My name is [Name] and I'm a researcher working on [project]. I'm part of a team that's trying to understand [broad framing — avoid leading the conversation].

> We're talking to [X type of people] to better understand [topic area — be deliberately vague here]. Your experience and perspective are genuinely valuable to us, and there are no right or wrong answers — we're here to learn from you, not test you.

> Before we start — I'd like to record this conversation so that I can focus on listening rather than writing. The recording will only be heard by our research team and will be kept securely. Your name won't be attached to anything we share outside this room. Is that OK with you?

> Do you have any questions before we start?"

### Warm-up question

Choose one that is relevant to the person's context but not related to the service yet. Its purpose is to help them relax and feel heard.

Options:
- "To start, can you tell me a little about yourself — what you do day to day?"
- "Can you tell me a bit about [relevant context: their caring role / their work / their neighbourhood]?"
- "Can you describe a typical [day/week] for you?"

## Projective or Hypothetical Questions (optional)

Use these if the topic is sensitive or if you want to surface values and emotions that are hard to articulate directly.

**Ideal scenario:**
> "If this service worked exactly as you needed it to, what would that look like?"

**Analogy:**
> "If this service was a person, how would you describe them?"

**Time travel:**
> "If you could go back to [moment], knowing what you know now, what would you do differently?"

**Magic wand:**
> "If you could change one thing about [service/experience], what would it be and why?"

## Sensitive Topic Guidance

For research involving potentially distressing topics (health conditions, bereavement, financial hardship, trauma):

- Brief participants fully before the session on the topic areas
- Have signposting information ready (support services, helplines) in case needed
- Give participants explicit permission to skip any question
- Allow the conversation to stop if the participant becomes distressed
- Debrief yourself and your team after difficult sessions
- Do not probe for distress — what you need is insight, not catharsis

## Example: Healthcare Appointment Research

**Research questions:**
1. What drives anxiety before and during hospital appointments?
2. How do patients currently manage uncertainty during waits?
3. What information do patients need, and when?

**Core topics:**

**Topic 1: Preparing for the appointment**
> "Can you walk me through what you did in the days before your last appointment?"

Probes:
- "Did you look anything up beforehand? What?"
- "How were you feeling in the days before you came in?"
- "Was there anything you were unsure about or worried about?"

**Topic 2: The day of the appointment**
> "Tell me about the day of your appointment — from when you left home to when you were seen."

Probes:
- "What was going through your mind as you were waiting?"
- "Did you know how long you'd be waiting? How did you find out?"
- "Was there anything that made the wait feel better or worse?"

**Topic 3: After the appointment**
> "After you saw the doctor, what happened next — from your point of view?"

Probes:
- "How did you feel leaving the appointment?"
- "Was there anything you were unclear about?"
- "Did you have to do anything as a follow-up? How did you know?"
