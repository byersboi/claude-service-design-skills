# Research Synthesis Workshop Guide

Step-by-step guide to running a research synthesis session that moves from raw observations to insight statements.

---

## Purpose

A synthesis workshop transforms research data — interview transcripts, observation notes, diary study outputs, field notes — into:
- Clustered themes
- Insight statements
- How Might We questions
- Input for personas and journey maps

Synthesis is the hardest and most important step in the research process. It is also the most commonly rushed.

---

## Pre-Workshop Preparation

**Time required to prepare:** 2–4 hours depending on volume of data.

### 1. Review all research materials

Read or re-watch all research sessions. For each session, write a brief summary:
- Participant code (not name)
- Key themes that emerged
- 3–5 most striking moments or quotes
- Any surprises or contradictions

### 2. Identify participants

Synthesis works best with 2–6 people. Too few: limited perspectives. Too many: the room gets too complex to manage.

Include: the people who conducted the research. Optionally include: a stakeholder or product owner who will benefit from observing synthesis.

### 3. Prepare the data

Transfer your most significant observations to individual sticky notes:
- One idea, observation, quote, or data point per sticky note
- Write clearly enough for others to read
- Avoid editorialising — write what was observed, not your interpretation
- Include participant code on the note if relevant to tracking patterns

Aim for: 5–10 sticky notes per interview session = 50–150 notes for a full research round.

### 4. Set up the space

**Physical:** Large wall or whiteboard space. Have sticky notes, markers, and the ability to rearrange freely.

**Digital (Miro/Mural):** Create a large working area. Pre-populate with all sticky notes before the session. Ensure all participants have editing access.

---

## Workshop Structure

### Part 1: Download (30–45 min)

**Purpose:** Share observations across the team before clustering. Prevents single-researcher bias.

**Activity:**
1. Each researcher takes 5–10 minutes to share the highlights from their sessions
2. Focus on: "What struck you? What surprised you? What did you not expect to find?"
3. Others listen and add their own reactions — "I heard similar things" / "I heard the opposite"
4. Do not analyse yet — this is about sharing and listening

**Output:** Shared awareness of the research material across the full team.

---

### Part 2: Cluster (45–60 min)

**Purpose:** Group similar observations together to identify patterns.

**Activity:**
1. Each person reads through their sticky notes silently and places them on the wall — no clustering yet
2. Silently begin moving notes into groups — without talking. Proximity = similarity.
3. When most notes are clustered, begin talking. Discuss disagreements about where notes belong.
4. Merge clusters that overlap. Split clusters that contain contradictory ideas.
5. Aim for 8–15 clusters.

**Facilitation notes:**
- Resist the urge to create the "right" clusters too early. Let the groupings emerge organically.
- A note that could belong in multiple clusters is usually pointing to a theme connection — explore it rather than forcing a placement.
- Watch for: the note no one knows where to put. It often contains the most interesting insight.

**Output:** 8–15 labelled clusters of related observations.

---

### Part 3: Name the clusters (20–30 min)

**Purpose:** Transform clusters from labels into insights.

A cluster name that describes its content ("Information problems") is a label.
A cluster name that captures the underlying truth ("Users don't lack information — they lack confidence that the information is right for them") is an insight.

**Activity:**
1. For each cluster, ask: "What is the non-obvious truth this cluster is pointing to?"
2. Write the insight as a complete sentence, not a label
3. Check: "Would someone outside this room know this from common sense alone?" If yes, push deeper.
4. Check: "Does this have implications for design?" If no, it may not yet be an insight.

**Insight statement formula:**
> "[Who] + [verb] + [what they do/think/feel] + because/when [non-obvious reason]"

**Output:** 8–15 insight statements.

---

### Part 4: How Might We (20–30 min)

**Purpose:** Reframe insights as design opportunities.

**Activity:**
1. For each insight statement, generate 2–4 HMW questions
2. Write each HMW on a separate sticky note
3. Aim for variety: one narrow HMW, one broad HMW, one transformational HMW per insight

**Output:** 20–50 HMW questions for use in ideation.

---

### Part 5: Synthesis outputs (30–45 min — may be separate session)

**Purpose:** Convert synthesis outputs into project artefacts.

**Activities:**
- Identify which clusters inform which persona archetypes
- Map insights to journey stages (which stage does each insight correspond to?)
- Identify the 3–5 most important insights for the design challenge
- Prioritise HMW questions for the ideation session

---

## After the Workshop

### Within 24 hours
- Photograph the affinity map (if physical) or export the digital board
- Write up the insight statements as a clean document
- Share with the wider project team
- Identify any gaps — what questions does the synthesis raise that the research didn't answer?

### Insight report
Produce a lightweight summary (2–4 pages or equivalent slide deck):
- Research questions and methods
- Key findings (3–5 headlines)
- Insight statements (with illustrative quotes)
- Implications for design
- Unanswered questions

---

## Common Synthesis Mistakes

**Moving to solution too quickly.** As insights emerge, teams jump to "we could build X." Name it and park it — synthesis is for understanding, not designing.

**Naming clusters too early.** Once a cluster has a name, it's hard to rearrange. Let clusters form for a while before naming them.

**Writing labels, not insights.** "Communication problems" is not an insight. "Patients don't lack information — they lack confidence that they have understood it correctly" is an insight.

**Losing the evidence.** Synthesis is an interpretive process. Track which observations support each insight so you can defend your interpretations.

**Excluding the difficult data.** Outliers and contradictions often contain the most important insights. Do not average them away.

## Timing Overview

| Phase | Duration |
|-------|---------|
| Pre-workshop preparation | 2–4 hours |
| Part 1: Download | 30–45 min |
| Part 2: Cluster | 45–60 min |
| Part 3: Name (insights) | 20–30 min |
| Break | 10 min |
| Part 4: HMW questions | 20–30 min |
| Part 5: Synthesis outputs | 30–45 min |
| **Total workshop** | **3–4 hours** |

For small research projects (6–8 interviews), compress to 2–3 hours. For large mixed-method research, allow a full day.
