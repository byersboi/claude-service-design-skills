# User Research

User research is the foundation of service design. It replaces assumptions about how users think, feel, and behave with evidence. Without research, you are designing for an imagined user — and the imagined user is almost always wrong in ways that matter.

Research in service design is primarily qualitative: seeking depth of understanding over breadth of numbers. The goal is not statistical significance but insight — the non-obvious truth that only emerges from sustained attention to real people in real contexts.

---

## When to Do Research

**Discover phase:** Open-ended research to understand the problem space. Who are the users? What is their experience? What matters to them? What are the hidden structures of their lives that this service intersects with?

**Define phase:** Targeted research to validate and deepen specific hypotheses emerging from early synthesis.

**Develop phase:** Evaluative research to test concepts and prototypes with users. Does this idea make sense? Does it work? What are the unintended consequences?

**Deliver phase:** Longitudinal or pilot research to evaluate the implemented service against design intent.

---

## Core Research Methods

### Semi-Structured Interviews

The workhorse of service design research. A conversation guided by a discussion guide — structured enough to cover key topics, flexible enough to follow what matters most to this specific person.

**When to use:** Understanding motivations, emotions, mental models, and past experiences. Not suitable for observing current behaviour.

**Sample size:** 6–15 interviews for most projects. Recruit until you reach saturation — when new interviews stop adding new themes.

**Duration:** 45–90 minutes per interview.

**Format:** In person preferred for rich observation; remote acceptable for accessibility and geographic range.

---

### Contextual Inquiry

Observing users in their natural environment while they go about relevant tasks. Combines observation with informal interview — you watch and ask questions in the moment.

**When to use:** Understanding actual behaviour (not reported behaviour), revealing workarounds, uncovering context that users take for granted and would never mention in an interview.

**Sample size:** 5–10 sessions.

**Duration:** 1–3 hours per session.

**The key principle:** "Master and apprentice." The user is the expert on their own experience; the researcher is the curious apprentice. Ask to be shown, not just told.

---

### Observation / Ethnographic Research

Watching without intervening. The researcher observes service interactions, waiting rooms, service environments, or everyday contexts without disrupting them.

**When to use:** Understanding the system as a whole, not just individual perspectives. Seeing what people do, not what they say they do.

**Fly on the wall:** Pure observation, no interaction.

**Shadowing:** Following a staff member or service user through a full service journey.

**Mystery shopping:** Experiencing the service as a user yourself.

---

### The Observer Effect

When participants are aware they are being observed or researched, they often change their behaviour — typically becoming more careful, more performative, or more aligned with what they think the researcher wants to see. This is known as the observer effect (and as the Hawthorne effect in workplace research contexts).

**Where it matters most:**

- **Contextual inquiry and shadowing:** Participants may perform tasks more carefully than usual, explain steps they would normally skip, or behave more "by the book" than they would in isolation.
- **Task-setting:** Asking a participant to "now do X while I watch" creates an artificial condition. The participant knows what outcome is expected and orients their behaviour toward it. What you observe tells you how people approach a task they have been asked to demonstrate — not necessarily how they approach it naturally.

**Mitigations:**
- Allow a settling-in period at the start of observation — spend 15–30 minutes building rapport before recording key observations
- Observe multiple participants and look for patterns that persist across individuals
- Ask explicitly: "Is this what you would normally do, or are you doing it differently because I'm here?"
- Complement observation with artefact review — completed forms, records, workaround documents — which reflect actual past behaviour rather than observed present behaviour
- In diary studies, remind participants regularly to capture what actually happens, not what they think they should be doing

**Note on task-set observations:** Flag in your synthesis notes which observations came from tasks you set versus natural behaviour. The two can be significantly different and lead to different design implications.

### Diary Studies

Participants record their own experiences, thoughts, and behaviours over a period of time — using written diaries, photos, audio, or video.

**When to use:** Understanding longitudinal experiences, behaviours that happen outside of observation windows, or topics that are difficult to discuss in interview (health behaviours, financial decisions).

**Duration:** 1–4 weeks.

**Sample size:** 8–15 participants.

**Prompts:** Provide daily or event-triggered prompts to guide entries. Balance structure (enough to produce analysable data) with openness (enough to capture what matters to participants).

---

### Cultural Probes

Designed provocations sent to participants to generate creative, subjective responses. Often includes materials like a disposable camera, a postcard to fill in, a map to annotate.

**When to use:** Sensitive topics, hard-to-reach audiences, when standard methods feel too clinical.

**Not for:** Generating quantitative data or systematic insight. Cultural probes generate stimuli for conversation — they require follow-up interviews to interpret.

---

### Vox Pops

Short, informal responses captured in public or service environments — typically 30–60 seconds per person. The name comes from the Latin *vox populi* ("voice of the people").

**When to use:** Gathering a wide range of quick perspectives from a broad population; understanding emotional reactions to a service or issue; generating stakeholder engagement material that conveys the range of people's lived experience; supplementing depth methods with breadth.

**How to run:** Approach people in or near the service environment — waiting areas, community spaces, service entrances. Ask one or two simple, open questions. Keep it brief and low-pressure: "I'm just doing a quick bit of research — do you have 30 seconds?" Record audio or video with consent, or take written notes.

**Sample size:** 15–30 responses for a meaningful range of perspectives.

**Limitations:** Vox pops surface breadth, not depth. The brevity of the format means participants cannot explore nuance, and responses reflect what people are willing to say on the spot — not necessarily their most considered views. Treat vox pops as a complement to depth methods, not a replacement. They are useful for early discovery and for bringing stakeholders close to user voices.

## Research Planning

### Research questions

Before designing any research activity, write down your research questions — what you need to understand, not what questions you will ask participants.

Research questions are for the team: "What motivates carers to seek outside help?"
Interview questions are for participants: "Can you tell me about a time when you first decided to ask for support?"

A focused research project has 3–6 research questions.

### Research plan

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| Research questions | What do we need to understand? |
| Methods | Which methods will answer which questions? |
| Participants | Who? How many? What criteria? |
| Recruitment approach | How will you find and recruit participants? |
| Timeline | When will research happen? |
| Roles | Who will conduct research? Who will observe? |
| Ethics | Consent, anonymisation, data storage |

### Recruitment

Recruit for diversity across the dimensions that matter for your service:
- Experience level (new users vs. long-term users)
- Channel (digital vs. non-digital)
- Outcome (successful users vs. those who struggled or abandoned)
- Demographics relevant to the service (age, geography, life situation)

Always over-recruit by 20–25% — dropouts are inevitable.

## Writing a Discussion Guide

A discussion guide is not a script. It is a structured set of topics and prompts to ensure key areas are covered while leaving room for conversation to go where it needs to.

### Structure

**Opening (5–10 minutes)**
- Introduce yourself and the project (without leading answers)
- Explain recording/consent
- Frame the conversation: "There are no right or wrong answers. We're here to learn from you."
- Warm-up question about the person (not the service) to build rapport

**Core topics (30–60 minutes)**
For each research question, prepare:
- An opening question (broad, open, retrospective)
- 2–3 follow-up prompts to deepen
- A projective or hypothetical if the topic is sensitive

**Closing (5–10 minutes)**
- "Is there anything you expected me to ask that I didn't?"
- "Is there anything else you want to tell me about this topic?"
- Explain next steps and how feedback will be used

### Question types

| Type | Example | When to use |
|------|---------|-------------|
| Open | "Can you tell me about your experience of..." | Open a topic |
| Retrospective | "Think about the last time you... What happened?" | Ground in a specific experience |
| Probing | "Can you say more about that?", "What do you mean by...?" | Deepen a response |
| Hypothetical | "If you could change one thing about..." | Explore preferences carefully |
| Projective | "If this service was a person, what would they be like?" | Surface emotions and values |
| Silent | [Pause after an answer] | Invite more — silence is often more powerful than a follow-up question |

**Avoid:**
- Leading questions ("Don't you find it frustrating when...")
- Double-barrelled questions ("Was it confusing and stressful?")
- Yes/no questions without a follow-up

## Research Synthesis

### Debrief

Run a research debrief within 24–48 hours of fieldwork while memories are fresh. Use a structured format:

1. **What struck you?** — Surface surprising or significant moments from each session
2. **What are you hearing repeatedly?** — Early patterns
3. **What are you not hearing that you expected?** — Significant absences
4. **What do you need to investigate further?**

### Affinity Mapping

Affinity mapping organises raw data (observations, quotes, insights) into themes through iterative clustering.

**Process:**
1. Transfer research observations to individual sticky notes — one idea per note
2. Without discussion, cluster similar notes together
3. Give each cluster a name that describes the underlying theme (not just the content)
4. Group clusters into higher-level categories
5. Walk through the map as a team — discuss, merge, split, rename

**Quality check:** Cluster names should be insight statements, not just labels. "Lack of information" is a label. "Patients manage anxiety by seeking information from any available source, even unofficial ones" is an insight.

### Insight Statements

An insight is a non-obvious truth about your users that has implications for design.

**Formula:** "[Who] + [verb] + [what they do/think/feel] + because/when [non-obvious reason]"

**Example:** "Carers delay seeking formal support because admitting they need help feels like admitting failure in their primary role."

Aim for 5–10 insights per research phase. Each insight should be:
- Grounded in multiple data points
- Non-obvious (if everyone knew it already, it is not an insight)
- Actionable (it should point toward a design opportunity)

## The Research Wall

A research wall is a physical or digital space where raw evidence — quotes, photos, observations, artefacts — is made continuously visible to the whole team throughout a project.

The research wall is not a synthesis output. It is a working environment. Its purpose is to keep the team close to the evidence at all times, so that design decisions can be traced directly to what was actually heard or seen.

**How to set one up:**

- Dedicate a section of physical wall space or a persistent digital board (Miro, Mural) to raw research evidence
- Pin verbatim quotes, photos from fieldwork, copies of documents participants showed you, participant sketches, and observations captured in the field
- Organise loosely by theme or research question, but resist over-organising — the wall should feel like evidence in progress, not a finished output
- Update it as research continues — add new observations in real time rather than batching them after a synthesis session
- Keep it accessible to the whole team, not just the researchers

**Why it works:** Decisions made close to the evidence are better decisions. When designers, delivery managers, and stakeholders can point to a specific quote or photo that supports a design choice, the decision has legitimate grounding. The research wall keeps the team honest about what was actually found, not what they remember or assume.

**Using the wall with stakeholders:** Before major project decisions, bring stakeholders to the wall. A stakeholder who has spent time in front of a research wall — reading quotes, looking at photos, seeing the volume and range of evidence — is harder to convince to override the research on the basis of internal assumptions. The wall becomes a persuasion tool as well as a team tool.

## Research Ethics

- Obtain informed consent before every session — explain what data will be collected, how it will be stored, and how it will be used
- Allow participants to withdraw at any time
- Anonymise data — use pseudonyms in synthesis and outputs
- Store data securely — do not share recordings or transcripts beyond the research team
- For vulnerable participants (children, people with health conditions, etc.): consult an ethics review process
- Provide appropriate incentives — typically £30–60 for an hour of a participant's time

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Master interview technique before anything else. Practise active listening: summarise what you heard, ask "can you say more about that?", sit with silence. Most junior researchers talk too much and listen too little.

**Mid-weight:** Push the synthesis further. Getting from observations to insight statements is the hardest step in the research process. Challenge yourself: "Is this actually non-obvious? Does this have design implications? What would a designer do differently if they knew this?"

**Senior / Lead:** Design research for the constraints of the project — not the ideal world. Know when to compress (guerrilla interviews, rapid synthesis) and when to invest (longitudinal studies, mixed methods). The most important research skill at this level is choosing the right methods for the question, not executing any single method perfectly.
