# Problem Framing

Problem framing is the discipline of defining the right problem before jumping to solutions. It sits in the Define phase of the Double Diamond — between discovery and ideation.

This is where most design projects go wrong. Teams rush from research to ideas without adequately defining what problem they are solving. The result is well-executed solutions to the wrong problem.

Good problem framing is specific, grounded in evidence, and opens up possibilities rather than prescribing a solution. It changes the team's orientation from "what should we build?" to "what problem should we be solving, and why?"

---

## Why Framing Matters

**The framing determines the solution space.** "How do we reduce call volume to the helpline?" generates very different ideas from "How do we give customers the confidence to resolve their issue without calling?" Both respond to the same business metric, but one frames the user as a problem to be managed; the other frames the experience as the thing to be improved.

**Bad framing generates good solutions to the wrong problem.** Teams can execute brilliantly against the wrong design challenge. The better your team's execution capability, the more damage a bad frame does.

**Reframing is a design act.** Proposing a better frame is often the most valuable thing a designer can contribute to a project.

---

## From Insight to Opportunity

The journey from raw research to a design challenge moves through several steps:

```
Observations → Themes → Insights → Opportunities (HMW) → Design challenge
```

**Observations:** What you saw and heard in research. Raw, specific, factual.
> "Three out of eight participants called the helpline because they couldn't find the answer on the website."

**Themes:** Patterns across multiple observations.
> "Users struggle to navigate the digital channel and default to calling."

**Insights:** The non-obvious truth behind the theme, with design implications.
> "Users don't call because they prefer talking — they call because finding information online feels uncertain and risky. Confidence, not preference, is the barrier."

**Opportunities (HMW):** The insight reframed as a design opportunity.
> "How might we give users confidence that they have found the right answer online?"

**Design challenge:** The focused brief for ideation.
> "Design a self-service experience that gives users certainty without requiring human validation."

## Tools and Techniques

### 1. The Five Whys

Drill from symptom to root cause by asking "why?" five times.

**Example:**

> **Problem:** High call volume to the helpline
>
> Why? → Users can't find answers on the website
> Why? → The website search returns too many irrelevant results
> Why? → Content is written in internal language, not in the words users search for
> Why? → Content is written by subject matter experts who don't think about how users search
> Why? → There is no process for validating content against user search behaviour

**Design challenge emerges at Why 4 or 5**, not at Why 1. Designing a better FAQ page addresses Why 3. Designing a content process that embeds user language addresses Why 5.

**Caution:** Five Whys can produce a single causal chain when the real cause is systemic. Use multiple chains and triangulate with research.

---

### 2. Point of View (POV) Statement

A POV statement captures the essential design challenge in a single sentence rooted in a specific user insight.

**Formula:**
> "[User] needs [need] because [insight]."

The "because" clause must be non-obvious — it is the insight that came from research, not the obvious explanation.

**Examples:**

| User | Need | Insight (because...) |
|------|------|---------------------|
| New carers | A way to ask for support without feeling judged | Asking for help is experienced as admitting failure in a role they feel obligated to fulfil perfectly |
| Hospital outpatients | A sense of control during waiting | Uncertainty about wait duration creates more anxiety than the wait itself |
| First-time benefit claimants | Reassurance that they are doing the right thing | The fear of making an error (and the unknown consequences) is more disabling than the process itself |

The POV statement is not a solution. "New carers need a free phone service" is not a POV statement — it is a solution masquerading as a need.

---

### 3. How Might We (HMW) Questions

HMW questions translate insights and POV statements into design opportunities. They are the bridge between Define and Develop.

**From POV to HMW:**
> POV: "New carers need a way to ask for support without feeling judged because asking is experienced as admitting failure."
>
> HMW: How might we make it easier for carers to ask for help without feeling like they've failed?
> HMW: How might we normalise the experience of needing support among people in caring roles?
> HMW: How might we design support-seeking as an act of strength rather than weakness?

Each insight should generate 3–5 HMW questions at different levels of ambition. The range creates variety in ideation.

**Levels of HMW:**
- **Too narrow:** "How might we add a reassuring message to the carer support form?" (Prescribes the solution)
- **About right:** "How might we make it easier for carers to ask for help without feeling like they've failed?" (Opens up the solution space)
- **Too broad:** "How might we improve mental health outcomes for carers?" (Too large — the solution space is infinite)

---

### 4. Reframing

Reframing challenges the assumptions embedded in an initial problem statement.

**Technique:** Take the initial brief or problem statement and ask:
- "Is this actually the right problem to solve?"
- "Whose problem is this framed as? What if it were framed differently?"
- "What assumptions are embedded in this framing?"
- "What is the underlying need or goal behind this problem?"
- "Who benefits from this framing, and who might it disadvantage?"

**Example:**

| Initial framing | Reframed as |
|----------------|------------|
| "How do we get more people to use the digital channel?" | "How do we make the digital channel worth using?" |
| "How do we reduce customer complaints?" | "How do we create the conditions where customers feel heard?" |
| "How do we help elderly users adopt new technology?" | "How do we design technology that doesn't require adoption because it fits into existing habits?" |
| "How do we increase compliance with medication?" | "How do we make the right behaviour the path of least resistance?" |

---

### 5. Assumptions Mapping

Identify the assumptions embedded in your current problem framing — and assess which are most uncertain and most critical.

**Process:**
1. Write your current problem statement
2. List all the assumptions it contains (things that must be true for the framing to be valid)
3. For each assumption, assess: How certain are we? How critical is it?
4. Focus investigation on assumptions that are both uncertain and critical

**Example assumptions for "We need to improve the GP referral process":**
- Patients are experiencing delays at the referral stage (not elsewhere)
- GPs are the primary bottleneck
- A process improvement will improve outcomes
- The current process is understood by all parties
- Patients and GPs share the same definition of "good referral"

Each assumption that turns out to be wrong is an opportunity to reframe.

## Design Challenge Statement

After using these tools, synthesise a design challenge statement — the focused brief that will drive ideation.

**Format:**
> "Design a [type of intervention] that [outcome for user] for [user segment] [constraint/context]."

Or more simply:
> "We want to [action] for [who] so that [outcome]."

**Example:**
> "We want to make it easier for new carers to identify and access the right support at the right time, without feeling like they need to have everything figured out first."

The design challenge should be:
- **Specific** — about a particular user segment and moment
- **Evidence-based** — traceable to research insights
- **Open** — not prescribing a solution
- **Inspiring** — motivating for the people who will ideate against it

## Common Mistakes

**Framing as a solution.** "How do we build a better app?" is a solution, not a problem. Strip the solution out and ask: "What problem would this app solve? For whom? Why?"

**Framing the organisation's problem, not the user's.** "How do we reduce operating costs?" is an organisational goal. Translate it: "What experience could we design that meets user needs more efficiently?"

**Too many design challenges.** Focus is a design decision. One clear design challenge is better than five vague ones.

**Skipping this phase.** Under time pressure, teams jump from research to ideation. This is where the value is lost. Even 2–3 hours of structured define work produces vastly better ideation.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Practice writing POV statements and HMW questions from your own research. The skill is in the "because" clause of the POV — that is where the insight lives. If the "because" is obvious, the insight is weak.

**Mid-weight:** Challenge the problem framing you are given. Most projects begin with a brief that embeds assumptions from the client's perspective. Your first job is to interrogate those assumptions, not accept them.

**Senior / Lead:** Frame reframing as a leadership act. When you propose a different problem frame to a client or senior stakeholder, you are not criticising their brief — you are doing design. Reframing requires evidence (from research), a compelling alternative, and the relational capital to be heard. Build all three.
