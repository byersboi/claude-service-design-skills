# Double Diamond

The Double Diamond is the foundational process framework for service design and design thinking. Developed by the British Design Council in 2005, it describes how design projects move through two cycles of divergent and convergent thinking — first to understand the problem space, then to develop and deliver a solution.

Understanding which phase you are in determines what activities to run, what outputs to produce, and what decisions to make.

## The Four Phases

```
    DISCOVER        DEFINE          DEVELOP         DELIVER
  (Diverge)      (Converge)       (Diverge)       (Converge)

  ◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇◇
  Open up         Narrow down      Open up         Narrow down
  possibilities   to the right     possibilities   to one
                  problem                          solution
```

### 1. Discover (Diverge)

**Goal:** Open up the problem space. Understand the world as it really is — not as you assume it to be.

Discover is about generating empathy and insight. Resist jumping to solutions. The output is raw material: observations, stories, data, tensions, and surprises.

**Key activities:**
- User research (interviews, observation, contextual inquiry)
- Desk research and secondary research
- Stakeholder interviews
- Safari / immersion visits
- Diary studies and cultural probes
- Expert interviews
- Competitor and analogous service review

**Output:** Research insights, observation notes, interview transcripts, photos, videos, stimuli

**Quality marker:** You have spoken with real users in their context, not just internal stakeholders or assumptions.

---

### 2. Define (Converge)

**Goal:** Synthesise what you learned and define the right problem to solve.

Define is about making sense of discovery. Move from raw data to insight, from insight to opportunity, from opportunity to a clear design challenge. The output is a problem statement that is specific, grounded in evidence, and opens up possibilities rather than prescribing a solution.

**Key activities:**
- Affinity mapping and thematic analysis
- Journey mapping (current state)
- Personas / user archetypes
- Stakeholder mapping
- How Might We (HMW) reframing
- Point of View (POV) statements
- Opportunity area identification
- Design principles

**Output:** Insight statements, How Might We questions, design brief, problem statement, personas, current-state journey map

**Quality marker:** The problem statement is based on evidence, not assumptions. The team is aligned on what problem they are solving and why.

---

### 3. Develop (Diverge)

**Goal:** Explore a wide range of possible solutions before committing to one.

Develop is about generative thinking. Generate many ideas, build rough prototypes, test quickly, and learn fast. Quantity before quality. The output is a range of concepts, some refined more than others.

**Key activities:**
- Ideation workshops (Crazy 8s, HMW brainstorming, SCAMPER)
- Concept development
- Co-design with users and stakeholders
- Service prototyping (role play, desktop walkthrough, storyboards)
- Rapid testing and feedback loops
- Service blueprinting (future state)
- Business model exploration

**Output:** Concept sketches, prototypes, storyboards, future-state blueprints, test insights

**Quality marker:** Multiple distinct concepts have been explored. Testing has happened with real users — not just internal review.

---

### 4. Deliver (Converge)

**Goal:** Refine, test, and implement the chosen solution.

Deliver is about making the solution real. Pilot, iterate, and hand over to the business for implementation. The output is a tested, validated solution with a clear implementation plan.

**Key activities:**
- Detailed service blueprinting
- Pilot planning and execution
- Stakeholder co-production
- Implementation roadmapping
- Measurement framework design
- Handover to operations or product teams
- Capability building

**Output:** Pilot results, refined service blueprint, implementation plan, measurement framework, handover documentation

**Quality marker:** The solution has been tested with real users in a real context. There is a clear owner and plan for implementation.

## Common Mistakes

**Jumping straight to solutions.** The most common failure in design practice. Teams often enter Develop before completing Discover and Define, solving the wrong problem with great execution.

**Spending too long in Discover.** Research paralysis is real. Set clear research questions before you start. Stop when you are hearing the same things repeatedly (saturation).

**Treating Define as a formality.** Synthesis is hard, time-consuming work. Rushing from research to ideation without proper synthesis leads to ideas that are not grounded in insight.

**Skipping Develop variety.** Teams often fall in love with the first idea. Force generation of at least three distinct concepts before evaluating any.

**Testing too late.** Prototyping and testing should happen throughout Develop — not only at the end. Test rough concepts early to avoid expensive late-stage pivots.

**Treating the process as linear.** The Double Diamond is iterative. It is normal and healthy to loop back. If a prototype reveals a fundamental misunderstanding, return to Define (or even Discover).

## Adapting for Different Contexts

**Short sprints (1–2 weeks):** Compress each phase. Use rapid research methods (guerrilla interviews, assumption-based proto-personas). Test with 3–5 users rather than 15–20.

**Large programmes:** Run multiple diamonds in parallel for different workstreams. Use a master diamond for the overall programme, with sub-diamonds for specific components.

**Incremental improvement (not greenfield):** Start with a light Discover phase focused on the existing service. Move quickly to Define to identify the highest-priority opportunity.

**Technology-led projects:** The process still applies but the Develop phase may involve more technical prototyping. Bring engineers in early during Develop — not only at Deliver.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Focus on understanding which phase you are in before taking action. Ask yourself: "Am I opening up possibilities or narrowing them down?" Use the phase activities lists as a menu.

**Mid-weight:** Use the phase gates (quality markers) to challenge your team. Push back on moving to the next phase until the current one is genuinely complete.

**Senior / Lead:** Use the Double Diamond to structure project timelines, resource allocation, and stakeholder communications. Brief clients and sponsors on which phase you are in and what decisions they need to make at each transition.
