# Adapting the Double Diamond

How to adapt the Double Diamond process for different project types, timescales, and organisational contexts.

---

## The Core Principle

The Double Diamond is a framework, not a prescription. The four phases — Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver — and the diverge/converge rhythm are always present. What changes is the depth, speed, and form of each phase depending on context.

The worst adaptation is skipping phases. The best adaptation is finding lighter, faster methods that preserve the integrity of each phase within tighter constraints.

---

## Adapting for Time Constraints

### 5-Day Design Sprint (Google Ventures format)

| Day | Phase | Activities |
|-----|-------|-----------|
| Monday | Define | Map the challenge, set a long-term goal, expert interviews, target selection |
| Tuesday | Develop (diverge) | Lighting demos (analogous inspiration), sketching, Crazy 8s |
| Wednesday | Develop (converge) | Concept decision, storyboard |
| Thursday | Deliver (prototype) | Build a realistic prototype (Keynote, Figma, or physical props) |
| Friday | Deliver (test) | 5 user interviews with the prototype |

**Note:** A 5-day sprint assumes the problem is already well-framed and the team has existing domain knowledge. If the problem is new or poorly understood, invest in Discover/Define before running the sprint.

---

### 2-Week Rapid Cycle

| Week | Phase | Activities |
|------|-------|-----------|
| Week 1, Days 1–2 | Discover | 6–8 rapid guerrilla interviews (20–30 min each), desk research |
| Week 1, Days 3–4 | Define | Affinity mapping, 3–5 insight statements, HMW questions |
| Week 1, Day 5 | Define → Develop | Design challenge statement, brief ideation warm-up |
| Week 2, Days 1–2 | Develop | Ideation (Crazy 8s, brainstorming), 2–3 concept sketches |
| Week 2, Days 3–4 | Develop → Deliver | Paper prototypes, 4–5 user tests (30 min each) |
| Week 2, Day 5 | Deliver | Synthesis, decisions, next steps presentation |

**Compromises made:** Fewer research participants, lighter synthesis, lower-fidelity prototypes. Accept these explicitly — do not pretend the insights are as robust as a full research phase.

---

### 3-Month Project

| Month | Phase | Activities |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| Month 1 | Discover | 12–15 interviews, 2–3 contextual inquiry sessions, stakeholder interviews, desk research |
| Month 1 → 2 | Define | Full affinity mapping, insight statements, personas, current-state journey map, design challenge |
| Month 2 | Develop | Multiple ideation sessions, 3 concept directions, co-design with users |
| Month 2 → 3 | Develop → Deliver | Prototype and test each concept (2 rounds), refine chosen concept |
| Month 3 | Deliver | Service blueprint, implementation plan, pilot design |

## Adapting for Agile / Sprint Environments

Service design in agile teams requires coordination between the design process and the delivery cycle.

### Discovery sprints

Run 1–2 dedicated discovery sprints at the start of a workstream. Use these to complete Discover and Define before the delivery team begins building.

**Typical discovery sprint (2 weeks):**
- Sprint 1: Research (interviews, contextual inquiry)
- Sprint 2: Synthesis, personas, journey map, design principles

### Dual-track agile

Run a design track slightly ahead of the delivery track:
- Design explores the next feature/area while delivery builds the current one
- Research and prototyping happen in design; implementation happens in delivery
- Design feeds insights, concepts, and validated prototypes into the delivery backlog

**Challenge:** Design track discoveries sometimes invalidate work already in progress in the delivery track. This tension is healthy — but requires clear communication protocols.

### Sprint-level prototyping

In an established delivery environment, lightweight prototyping and testing should happen within each sprint before work is finalised — not only at the end of a project.

## Adapting for Incremental Service Improvement

When redesigning an existing service (not building from scratch):

**Compress Discover:** Focus research on the specific pain points or moments you are improving. You do not need a full end-to-end discovery if the scope is bounded.

**Use analytics as a Discover accelerant:** Existing quantitative data (completion rates, drop-off points, complaint volumes) can direct research to the highest-priority areas before fieldwork begins.

**Light-touch Define:** If the team has deep domain knowledge and existing research, synthesis may be a 1-day workshop rather than a 2-week process.

**Iterate in Develop:** For incremental improvements, a single concept direction is often appropriate. Focus Develop on making the chosen direction as good as it can be through multiple small test-and-iterate cycles.

## Adapting for Complex Programmes

Large transformation programmes may involve multiple interconnected services, many stakeholder groups, and extended timelines.

### Programme-level diamond

Use a high-level diamond for the overall programme:
- Programme Discover: System-level research — how do the components interconnect? What are the systemic failure points?
- Programme Define: System map, cross-cutting insights, programme design principles
- Programme Develop: Overall service model design
- Programme Deliver: Sequenced implementation across workstreams

### Workstream-level diamonds

Each workstream (e.g., specific service, specific user group, specific channel) runs its own diamond in parallel, aligned to programme-level direction.

### Governance considerations

- Phase gate reviews at programme level, not workstream level
- Cross-workstream learning sessions at Define → Develop and Develop → Deliver transitions
- Shared design principles to maintain coherence across workstreams

## Adapting for Low Research Access

In some contexts — tight timescales, access restrictions, sensitive populations — it is not possible to conduct ideal-volume research.

### Minimum viable Discover

- 5 user interviews (rather than 12–15)
- 2–3 contextual inquiry sessions or shadowing observations
- Desk research and existing data (complaints, analytics, previous research)
- Explicit assumption logging: "We believe X — this is untested"

**Label low-confidence assumptions clearly in all outputs.** Design from assumptions if you must — but be honest that you are doing so, and build in early validation.

### Guerrilla research

Recruit quickly and informally: intercept users at point of service, use community contacts, leverage existing events.

- Keep sessions shorter (20–30 minutes instead of 60–90)
- Narrow the scope of each session to the most critical questions
- Aim for depth of insight over breadth of sample

---

## Signs You Are Adapting Badly

**Skipping research entirely:** "We already know our users" is usually not true. Assumptions replace evidence. The project will eventually discover this — often at delivery, when it is most expensive.

**Collapsing Define and Develop:** Moving from research to ideation without synthesis produces ideas disconnected from insight. Even a half-day synthesis session is better than none.

**Treating the process as a compliance exercise:** Running a "Discover phase" that consists of two internal stakeholder interviews and a desk review is not Discover — it is optics. Be honest about what the project is actually doing.

**Dropping the close of Deliver:** Implementation planning and measurement are not bonus activities. A designed service that is not implemented or measured is a report, not a service.
