# Service Standards

Service standards are frameworks for evaluating whether a service works well for the people who use it. They shift the question from "does this service function?" to "does this service genuinely serve people?"

Two frameworks are most widely used in UK service design practice: **Lou Downe's 15 Principles of Good Service Design** and the **GDS Service Standard**. They are complementary — the 15 Principles focus on the user experience of a service; the GDS Standard focuses on how a service is built and run.

---

## The 15 Principles of Good Service Design

Developed by Lou Downe, former Head of Design at the Government Digital Service, and published in the book *Good Services* (2020). These principles define what a service needs to do to work well for users. They apply to any service — digital, physical, or blended.

Use them as a diagnostic: for each principle, ask "does our service do this?" and "where does it fall short?"

---

### 1. Be easy to find

A user with no prior knowledge of the task they are trying to complete must be able to find the service unaided. Someone who wants to "learn to drive" must be able to find their way to "apply for a driving licence" without already knowing that is what they need.

**Design implication:** Think about how users describe their goal — not how your organisation describes the service. Search terms, signposting, and entry points should all match user language, not internal naming conventions.

---

### 2. Clearly explain its purpose

The purpose of the service must be immediately clear to a user who has never used it before. They must understand what the service will do for them and how it will work before they commit to starting.

**Design implication:** The start page or entry point must answer: what is this, who is it for, what will it do for me? This information cannot be buried.

---

### 3. Set the expectations a user has of it

The service must clearly explain what the user needs to provide, what the service will do in return, how long it will take, what it will cost, and any eligibility restrictions — before the user begins.

**Design implication:** Unmet expectations are one of the most common causes of user frustration and support contacts. Tell people what to expect upfront, even if it is bad news.

---

### 4. Enable users to complete the outcome they set out to do

A service helps the user achieve a goal — start a business, move house, get a benefit — in as seamless a sequence of steps as possible. This starts from the moment they consider doing something and ends only when their goal is fully achieved, including any post-completion support.

**Design implication:** Map the full journey from the user's goal, not from the organisation's process. Where does the service hand off? What happens then? Users do not experience handoffs as separate — they experience them as failure.

---

### 5. Work in a way that is familiar

People base their understanding of the world on previous experiences. If there is an established convention that benefits users, follow it. But not all conventions benefit users — some exist for organisational convenience. Avoid conventions that create friction, confusion, or exclusion.

**Design implication:** Do not innovate for its own sake. Familiar patterns reduce cognitive load. Deviate from convention only when there is a clear user benefit.

---

### 6. Require no prior knowledge to use

A service should not assume any prior knowledge from the user — no knowledge of the organisation, the process, the terminology, or the system. The user should be able to complete the service knowing only their own goal.

**Design implication:** Audit every piece of jargon, every unexplained acronym, every step that assumes the user knows what comes next. If a user needs to read a guide before using the service, the service has failed this principle.

---

### 7. Be agnostic to organisational structures

The service must work in a way that does not expose the user to the internal structures of the organisation providing it. Users should never need to understand which department, team, or system they are dealing with in order to complete their goal.

**Design implication:** This is the hardest principle for large organisations. Internal silos create external seams — the user experiences them as inconsistency, dead ends, and having to repeat themselves. Cross-functional design is a structural requirement, not a preference.

---

### 8. Require as few steps as possible

A good service minimises the burden on the user. This sometimes means proactively meeting user needs before they ask — for example, pre-populating known information, or anticipating the next step and surfacing it automatically.

**Design implication:** Every step the user must take is a step they might abandon. Audit each step: is this genuinely necessary? Who is this step for — the user, or the organisation?

---

### 9. Be consistent throughout

The service should look, feel, and work like a single service across all channels and touchpoints. Language, visual style, and interaction patterns should be consistent whether the user is on a website, on the phone, in person, or receiving a letter.

**Design implication:** Inconsistency signals incompetence and erodes trust. Establish shared patterns, language, and standards across all channels — including legacy channels that often get forgotten.

---

### 10. Have no dead ends

Every user should be directed to a clear outcome, regardless of whether they are eligible for the service. No user should be left stranded without knowing what to do next.

**Design implication:** Design for the ineligible user, not just the happy path. When someone cannot use the service, where do they go? What do they do? A dead end forces them to call, resulting in a support contact that could have been designed out.

---

### 11. Be usable by everyone equally

The service must be usable by everyone who needs it, regardless of their circumstances, abilities, or background. No user should be systematically less able to use the service than any other.

**Design implication:** Accessibility is a legal requirement in the UK (WCAG 2.1 AA minimum for public sector services) and a design requirement for all services. Test with users who have access needs, low digital literacy, limited English, or other barriers. If it works for the most excluded user, it works for everyone.

---

### 12. Encourage the right behaviours from users and staff

The service should encourage behaviours that are safe, productive, and mutually beneficial. For users, it should not create habits that could cause harm in other contexts — for example, normalising the sharing of personal data without understanding how it will be used. For staff, incentive structures should never reward poor service — for example, measuring call handlers on call length in ways that incentivise cutting conversations short.

**Design implication:** Design for the whole system, not just the interface. What behaviours does this service create? What does it incentivise? Who might be harmed by the norms it establishes?

---

### 13. Respond to change quickly

When a user's circumstances change, the service should respond consistently and immediately across all channels. If a user updates their contact details online, those details should be reflected in the face-to-face service, the postal correspondence, and the phone system.

**Design implication:** Stale data and siloed systems create a service that contradicts itself. Single sources of truth, real-time data synchronisation, and joined-up back-end systems are service design requirements — not just technical ones.

---

### 14. Clearly explain why a decision has been made

When a decision is made within a service — eligibility, outcome, next step — the reason must be clearly communicated at the point it is made. The user should also be given a route to challenge or appeal the decision.

**Design implication:** Unexplained decisions are experienced as arbitrary and unfair, even when they are neither. Transparency about decision-making builds trust. The right to challenge is not just good design — in many contexts it is a legal requirement.

---

### 15. Make it easy to get human assistance

There should always be a clear, easy route for a user to speak to a person if they need to. Digital-first does not mean digital-only.

**Design implication:** Designing out human contact to reduce costs often increases it — users who cannot complete a digital service call instead, at greater cost and frustration. Offer human assistance at the point of need. Do not bury the phone number.

## The GDS Service Standard

The Government Digital Service (GDS) Service Standard (gov.uk/service-manual/service-standard) sets out 14 criteria that UK government services must meet. Where the 15 Principles focus on the experience of a service, the GDS Standard focuses on how a team builds and runs it.

### Why the Standard exists: the scale problem

Government is the oldest and largest service provider in the UK. The scale is hard to overstate:

- Approximately **10,000 services** across government
- **25 departments**
- **418,343 people** delivering those services

> "Most of government is mostly service design most of the time."
> — Matt Edgar, Head of Design, NHS Digital

The vast majority of these services were not designed for the internet — they were designed for paper, telephone, or face-to-face delivery, and then digitised without being redesigned. The Service Standard exists to establish shared quality criteria at that scale: not just for individual flagship services, but across everything.

### From the Digital Service Standard to the Government Service Standard

The original **Digital Service Standard** focused specifically on digital delivery — it applied to online transactions above a certain transaction volume threshold. This created a structural gap: it applied well to high-volume digital services, but left the majority of government services — lower-volume services, policy delivery, telephony, face-to-face services — without a common quality framework.

In 2018, GDS began the shift to the **Government Service Standard** — broadening the scope from digital services to all government services, and from transactions to the whole problem a user is trying to solve. The key changes in emphasis were:

| From | To |
|------|----|
| Digital Service Standard | Government Service Standard |
| Applies to digital transactions | Applies to all government services |
| Focused on individual transactions | Focused on whole problems users are trying to solve |
| Primarily a technology quality standard | A service design quality standard |

Three themes were foregrounded in the revised standard:

- **Understand your context** — know the landscape of the service: what else exists, what users are already doing, what the constraints are
- **Think big but take small steps** — design toward a whole-service vision, but deliver in iterative increments that each provide value
- **Join up across channels** — the service must work coherently whether the user is online, on the phone, or in person

The standard was then made public, enforced, and subject to ongoing iteration — following the same principles it asks services to embody.

### The 14 criteria (summary)

| # | Criterion |
|---|-----------|
| 1 | Understand users and their needs |
| 2 | Solve a whole problem for users |
| 3 | Provide a joined-up experience across all channels |
| 4 | Make the service simple to use |
| 5 | Make sure everyone can use the service |
| 6 | Have a multidisciplinary team |
| 7 | Use agile ways of working |
| 8 | Iterate and improve frequently |
| 9 | Create a secure service that protects users' privacy |
| 10 | Define what success looks like and publish performance data |
| 11 | Choose the right tools and technology |
| 12 | Make new source code open |
| 13 | Use and contribute to open standards, common components and patterns |
| 14 | Operate a reliable service |

### Service assessments

Government services must pass a service assessment at Alpha, Beta, and Live stages. Assessors review the team against the 14 criteria. Failing an assessment does not end the project — it identifies what must be improved before the next stage.

**Preparing for an assessment:**
- Keep a decision log — assessors ask why choices were made, not just what was built
- Evidence user research at every stage — not just at discovery
- Show iteration — demonstrate that you tested, learned, and changed based on findings
- Be honest about what you do not yet know — assessors respond better to acknowledged uncertainty than to overclaiming

## Using Standards as a Design Tool

Standards are most useful when applied throughout the design process — not just at the end as an evaluation checklist.

### At Discovery
Use the 15 Principles to frame research questions:
- Principle 1 (easy to find): "How are users currently finding this service? Who is not finding it?"
- Principle 6 (no prior knowledge): "What do users assume they need to know before they can start?"
- Principle 10 (no dead ends): "What happens to users who are ineligible? Where do they go?"

### At Define
Use the principles to sharpen problem statements:
- "Users cannot complete their goal because the service assumes prior knowledge of our internal structure" (Principle 7)
- "Staff are incentivised to process cases quickly rather than to resolve users' underlying goals" (Principle 12)

### At Develop
Use the principles to evaluate concepts before building:
- Does this concept work for someone with no prior knowledge? (Principle 6)
- Does it require the user to understand which team they are dealing with? (Principle 7)
- What happens if the user is ineligible? (Principle 10)

### At Deliver
Use both the 15 Principles and the GDS Standard as a pre-launch audit. Score each principle: fully met / partially met / not met. For anything partially or not met, define the minimum viable fix before launch and the full fix for the next iteration.

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Learn the 15 Principles as a lens for reviewing your own work. Before presenting a design, walk through the principles and ask: does this meet principle 6 (no prior knowledge)? Does it meet principle 10 (no dead ends)? Self-critique before someone else does it.

**Mid-weight:** Use the principles to challenge briefs and assumptions from the outset. When a stakeholder describes what they want built, map it against the principles before agreeing to the brief. "This proposal may struggle with Principle 7 — users will need to know which team to contact at this point. Let's explore that."

**Senior / Lead:** Use the standards to shape organisational behaviour, not just individual projects. The hardest principles to meet (7, 12, 13) require structural change — cross-functional teams, joined-up data, shared channels. Advocate for these at programme and leadership level. A service cannot meet these principles through design alone.
