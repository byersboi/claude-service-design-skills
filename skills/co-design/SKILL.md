# Co-design

Co-design is a design approach where the people who will use, deliver, or be affected by a service are actively involved in creating it — not just consulted or observed. The distinction is meaningful: in co-design, users are treated as experts in their own experience and as partners in the creative process.

The phrase "designing with, not for" captures the essential shift. Standard design research treats users as data sources. Co-design treats them as co-authors.

---

## Why Co-design

**Better outcomes.** People who experience a service daily have knowledge that no amount of external observation can fully capture. Solutions designed with this knowledge embedded are more likely to work in practice.

**Greater legitimacy.** Services and systems that have been developed with the people they affect have stronger community buy-in and are more likely to be adopted and sustained.

**Ethical imperative.** In contexts where services affect people's lives significantly — health, social care, housing, criminal justice — involving those people in design is not just pragmatic but an ethical obligation.

**Implementation readiness.** When delivery staff and users have been involved in designing a service, they understand it better and are more likely to implement it faithfully.

---

## Co-design vs. Standard Research

| Standard research | Co-design |
|------------------|-----------|
| Researcher observes / interviews users | Users generate ideas alongside researchers |
| Users as subjects / informants | Users as partners / experts |
| Insights extracted from users | Insights developed with users |
| Design happens after research | Design happens with users throughout |
| Findings shared with users (if at all) | Users shape findings and outputs |

Co-design does not replace user research — it extends it. The rigour of observational and interview research is essential. Co-design adds a generative, creative dimension.

---

## The Co-design Spectrum

Co-design exists on a spectrum from light involvement to full user leadership:

```
Inform → Consult → Involve → Collaborate → User-led
```

**Inform:** Share findings with users. No input into design.
**Consult:** Ask for feedback on decisions already made.
**Involve:** Users contribute to generating ideas and solutions.
**Collaborate:** Users and designers work together throughout.
**User-led:** Users lead the design process with designer support.

Most service design practice sits in the "involve" to "collaborate" range. Be clear about where you are on this spectrum — and honest with participants about what influence they will have. False promises of co-design followed by decisions made elsewhere destroy trust.

## Preparing for Co-design

### Who to involve

- **Users with direct experience** of the service — including those who have had negative experiences, those who have struggled, those who have been excluded
- **Front-line staff** — who have contextual knowledge no manager possesses
- **Carers and proxies** — people who manage services on behalf of others
- **Community representatives** — particularly for public services

**Diversity is not optional.** Co-design with only the most articulate, available, or digitally literate users produces a partial picture. Actively recruit for diversity — in experience, in capability, in geography, in outcome.

### Recruitment

- Pay participants fairly for their time (standard for user research applies equally here — typically £30–60 per hour equivalent)
- Provide accessible venues and formats — consider transport, childcare, timing, language
- Brief participants clearly: what they will be doing, how long, how their input will be used
- Be honest about the constraints: "We will use your input to inform our design, but we cannot promise that every idea will be implemented"

### Supporting participants to contribute

Not everyone arrives ready to brainstorm. Warm-up activities, creative stimulus, and permission to be wrong are essential.

Design for the least confident participant in the room. If the activity works for someone with low confidence or limited literacy, it will work for everyone.

## Generative Activities

Co-design uses creative activities that help participants express ideas and experiences they might struggle to articulate verbally.

### Card-based activities

Provide participants with cards containing images, phrases, or service scenarios. Ask them to sort, rank, arrange, or respond to the cards.

**Useful for:** Prioritising needs, identifying preferred journeys, exploring values.

**Example:** "Here are 12 cards describing different moments in a hospital visit. Can you arrange them from the one that matters most to the one that matters least?"

### Making activities

Invite participants to sketch, build, or construct representations of their ideal service.

**Paper prototyping with users:** Give participants blank paper and pens and ask them to sketch what a better version of the service would look like.

**Model building:** Use modelling clay, Lego, or craft materials to build physical representations of service spaces or experiences.

**Why it works:** Making externalises thinking. Creating a physical or visual artefact gives participants something to discuss and explain, rather than having to generate articulate verbal responses on demand.

### Future scenarios

Ask participants to describe or act out a future vision of the service.

**"Newspaper from the future":** "It's two years from now and this service has been redesigned and works brilliantly. Write the newspaper headline about it." Invite people to draw or write the article.

**Role play:** Give participants a scenario card and invite them to act out how they would ideally experience the service.

### Mapping activities

Ask participants to map their own experience — journey maps, stakeholder maps, or day-in-the-life maps — from their own perspective.

**Participant journey mapping:** Give participants a blank journey map template and ask them to fill in their own experience. Then use this as a basis for discussion.

### Prioritisation activities

Dot voting, card sorting, and 2x2 matrices work well in co-design because they are accessible, quick, and generate visible outputs.

## Running a Co-design Session

### Session structure

**1. Welcome and context (10–15 min)**
- Introduce the project and the team
- Explain why the participant is there and how their input will be used
- Set expectations honestly — particularly around what will and won't be acted on
- Establish ground rules: there are no right or wrong answers; everyone's experience is valuable

**2. Warm-up (10–15 min)**
- An easy, low-stakes activity that builds confidence
- Not directly about the service — a general creative or social activity
- Purpose: break the ice, establish that this is a participatory session, not a lecture

**3. Core activity: exploring experience (20–30 min)**
- Generative activities focused on current experience
- Mapping, storytelling, card-based activities
- Goal: surface the lived experience of the current service

**4. Core activity: generating ideas (20–30 min)**
- Generative activities focused on future possibilities
- Sketching, scenario building, "newspaper from the future"
- Goal: surface ideas and visions for a better service

**5. Prioritisation and feedback (15–20 min)**
- Dot voting, ranking, or structured discussion
- "If we could only do one thing, what would it be?"
- Document reasoning, not just outcomes

**6. Close and next steps (10 min)**
- Summarise what was produced
- Explain what will happen next and how input will be used
- Thank participants; confirm payment process

## Ethics and Power

Co-design involves real people sharing real experiences, often of difficult or sensitive services. Take the ethics seriously.

**Informed consent.** Participants must understand what they are consenting to: being in a session, having their contributions recorded, how the outputs will be used.

**Managing expectations.** Be explicit about the limits of co-design. Participants should not leave sessions believing that every idea will be implemented. Explain the design process and where their input sits within it.

**Avoiding tokenism.** Involving people in one workshop and then designing without them is tokenism. If you are committed to co-design, build participation into the whole project, not just one session.

**Working with vulnerable populations.** When co-designing with children, people with serious health conditions, people in crisis, or other vulnerable groups: seek ethical review, provide appropriate support, and design activities that do not require participants to relive trauma.

**Feedback loop.** Tell participants what happened as a result of their input. When participants never hear back, it reinforces the belief that their views do not matter.

## Common Mistakes

**Using co-design language without co-design practice.** Saying you are "co-designing" while making all the significant design decisions internally is not co-design — and participants will notice.

**Recruiting only the most articulate and available.** The people most willing to attend workshops are often not representative of the service population. Invest in reaching the harder-to-reach.

**Treating co-design as the only input.** Co-design generates valuable input but is not a substitute for observation, analytics, and other research methods.

**Not acting on what participants produce.** If the design does not reflect participants' input, explain why. Unexplained divergence destroys trust.

**Not paying participants.** Asking people to contribute unpaid to design a service that will be delivered commercially is extractive. Pay fairly.

---

## Level Guidance

**Junior:** Focus on creating safety. The quality of co-design sessions depends almost entirely on whether participants feel safe to be honest. Learn warm-up and icebreaker facilitation. Practise making people feel genuinely heard, not just processed.

**Mid-weight:** Design activities that work without strong verbal skills. Many co-design participants are not comfortable in group discussion. Visual, tactile, and written activities reach people who would not speak up in a workshop.

**Senior / Lead:** Embed co-design in the project governance, not just the research phase. Advocate for ongoing user involvement throughout Develop and Deliver — not a single consultation exercise. Build lived-experience input into design critiques, prototype testing, and pilot evaluation.
