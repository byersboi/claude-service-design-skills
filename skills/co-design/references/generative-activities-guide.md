# Generative Activities Guide

Detailed instructions for co-design activities, including materials, timing, and facilitation notes.

---

## Activity 1: Experience Timeline

**Purpose:** Help participants map their own experience of a service over time, in their own words and framing.

**Time:** 30–40 minutes

**Materials:** Large paper or card (A3+), marker pens, sticky notes, a printed timeline template (optional)

**Instructions to participants:**
> "On this piece of paper, draw a line from left to right. The left end is the beginning of your experience with [service] and the right end is where you are now — or the end of the experience. I'd like you to mark out the important moments along that line — the things that stand out, whether they were positive, negative, or just significant. You can write, draw, or use sticky notes. This is your journey — there's no right or wrong way to do it."

**Facilitation notes:**
- Walk alongside participants as they work. Ask gentle questions: "What happened next?" "How were you feeling at that point?"
- Don't structure the timeline for them — their framing is the data
- Look for: moments they linger on, moments they skip quickly, emotional peaks and troughs

**Debrief:** Ask participants to walk you through their timeline. What stands out? What would they change?

## Activity 2: Dream Service Cards

**Purpose:** Generate ideas for a better service through creative prompts.

**Time:** 25–35 minutes

**Materials:** A set of image cards (12–20 images of diverse spaces, interactions, and objects), A4 paper, pens

**Instructions to participants:**
> "These cards show lots of different places, interactions, and objects. I'd like you to choose 2–3 that remind you in some way of how a better version of [service] could feel — they don't need to be literally related. For each one you choose, can you explain what it represents for you?"

**Facilitation notes:**
- Accept unexpected connections — they often contain the most interesting insights
- Follow up: "What is it about [image] that you connect to? What would that look like in the service?"
- Document which images are chosen and the reasoning

**Debrief:** What patterns emerge across participants? What qualities do they consistently reach for?

## Activity 3: Newspaper from the Future

**Purpose:** Generate visions of a successful future service without constraints.

**Time:** 20–30 minutes

**Materials:** A printed "newspaper template" (header, columns, space for a headline and article), pens

**Instructions to participants:**
> "Imagine it's two years from now. [Service] has been redesigned and is working brilliantly. A local newspaper has written a story about it. Can you write — or sketch — the headline and a few sentences of the article? What has changed? What are people saying about it?"

**Facilitation notes:**
- Encourage participants to include direct quotes from imaginary users in their articles
- If someone struggles: "What's the headline? Just one sentence — what's the most important thing that changed?"
- Look for: specific changes they mention, emotions they describe, who benefits

**Debrief:** Share headlines. What are the common themes? What surprises you?

## Activity 4: Worst Possible Service

**Purpose:** Surface problems and priorities through inversion. Lowers stakes and increases creativity.

**Time:** 20–25 minutes

**Materials:** Sticky notes, pens

**Part 1 — Generate bad ideas (10 min):**
> "I'd like us to design the absolute worst possible version of [service]. How would you make it as frustrating, confusing, and unhelpful as possible? Write each terrible idea on a separate sticky note."

**Part 2 — Reverse (10 min):**
> "Now look at each bad idea. What would its opposite be? If the worst idea is [X], what would a service that did the reverse look like?"

**Facilitation notes:**
- Part 1 is deliberately playful — laugh at the worst ideas. This creates safety.
- Part 2 is where the real value lies. The reversals often surface ideas that wouldn't emerge from direct brainstorming.
- Push for specificity in both directions: "How would you make it even worse?" → "What would the exact opposite of that look like?"

## Activity 5: Journey Mapping with Participants

**Purpose:** Build a first-person account of the service experience.

**Time:** 40–60 minutes

**Materials:** Printed journey map template (blank, with stage prompts), sticky notes in 3 colours (actions, thoughts, feelings), pens

**Instructions to participants:**
> "This template represents your journey with [service]. Working from left to right, I'd like you to fill in the stages of your experience. Use the [colour] sticky notes for what you did, [colour] for what you were thinking, and [colour] for how you felt. Don't worry about filling every cell — focus on the parts that feel most significant."

**Facilitation notes:**
- Start participants with the easiest part — usually the actions row
- Sit back and observe. Only ask questions when they pause or seem stuck.
- Take photos at each stage before participants add to the map
- Watch for: where they linger, where they add most sticky notes, where they fall silent

**Debrief:** Walk through the map together. "What stands out to you looking at this?" "If you could change one thing, what would it be?"

## Activity 6: Card Sort — Priorities

**Purpose:** Identify what matters most to participants, and reveal trade-offs.

**Time:** 20–30 minutes

**Materials:** A set of printed cards (10–15), each describing a feature, service attribute, or value. Optionally: a sorting template (e.g., must have / nice to have / don't need)

**Instructions to participants:**
> "Here are [X] things a service like [service] might offer. I'd like you to sort them into three piles: things that are essential (you can't imagine the service without them), things that would be nice but not essential, and things you don't particularly care about."

**Facilitation notes:**
- The sorting reveals relative priorities — more valuable than asking "how important is X?"
- Watch for: items that participants are uncertain about (they'll often hold them, look at them, put them in a pile, then move them)
- After sorting: "Are there any items in the 'nice to have' pile that are almost essential?"
- Probe the "don't care" pile: "Is there anything here that you actively don't want?"

**Card content examples:**
- "Being seen at my appointment time"
- "Being treated like an individual, not a number"
- "Clear information about what happens next"
- "The ability to sort things out online without calling"
- "Friendly staff who explain things clearly"
- "Being kept updated automatically"
- "A fast process with minimal paperwork"

## Activity 7: Role Play — Future Service

**Purpose:** Experience a potential future service interaction and surface how it feels.

**Time:** 20–40 minutes

**Materials:** A brief scenario card (describing the participant's situation and goal), simple props if available

**Instructions to participants:**
> "I'm going to play the role of someone from [service]. You are [brief character description from scenario card]. Let's act out you coming to [service] for help. Just respond naturally — you don't need to stay strictly in character. We'll pause and reflect as we go."

**Facilitation notes:**
- Brief clearly: "There are no wrong answers. Say what your character would naturally say or do."
- Pause at key moments: "How are you feeling right now?" "What would you naturally do next?"
- After the role play: "What worked well? What felt off? What would you change?"
- Do not expect theatrical commitment — the value is in the reactions, not the performance

## Activity 8: Empathy Mapping in Groups

**Purpose:** Build a shared understanding of a user's perspective across the team.

**Time:** 30–40 minutes

**Materials:** Large paper or whiteboard divided into four quadrants (Says / Thinks / Does / Feels), sticky notes, pens

**Instructions (for groups — participants describe a person they know who uses the service):**
> "Think of someone in your life who uses [service] — a parent, a friend, a colleague. I'm not asking you to speak for all users — just about this one specific person. As we work through these quadrants, think about what they would say, think, do, and feel."

**Facilitation notes:**
- This works particularly well with front-line staff and carers — who spend significant time with users but are rarely asked to centre their perspective
- Push for specific examples: "What does your mum actually say when she calls the helpline?"
- Look for tensions: what they say vs. what they think (often the most revealing quadrant)

## Selecting Activities for Your Session

| Activity | Best for | Time needed | Group size |
|----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Experience timeline | Understanding lived experience | 30–40 min | Individual or pairs |
| Dream service cards | Generating aspirational visions | 25–35 min | Individual or small group |
| Newspaper from the future | Generating future vision | 20–30 min | Individual or pairs |
| Worst possible service | Surfaces problems, breaks inhibition | 20–25 min | Any |
| Journey mapping with participants | Detailed experience mapping | 40–60 min | Individual |
| Card sort — priorities | Identifying priorities | 20–30 min | Individual |
| Role play — future service | Testing interactions | 20–40 min | Pairs |
| Empathy mapping in groups | Building organisational empathy | 30–40 min | Groups of 4–8 |
